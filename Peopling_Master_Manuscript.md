# **CHAPTER 1:**  INSIDE YOUR HEAD

## How You Actually Make Decisions (And Why You're Wrong About It)

## The Illusion of Control

You're in a meeting. You're discussing a decision. Someone proposes an idea. You feel yourself leaning toward it or against it. You think you're evaluating it logically.

You feel like you're deciding.

But here's what's actually happening: Your brain decided *before* you were conscious of it. Your conscious mind is just catching up, trying to make sense of what your brain already did.

Then—and this is the critical part—your brain creates a *narrative* about why you decided. A story that feels true. A rationale that makes sense.

You're not making the decision. You're observing it. And then you're narrating it.

This isn't weakness or failure. This is how human brains work. Understanding it changes everything about how you lead.

## How Your Brain Actually Works

Your brain is fundamentally a prediction machine.

It's not passively receiving information and analyzing it. It's constantly predicting what will happen next. It's predicting what things should look like. It's predicting how people will behave.

When you see a tree, your brain isn't analyzing pixels. It's predicting "tree" based on pattern. When you hear your colleague's tone in a meeting, your brain isn't analyzing sound waves. It's predicting their emotional state and intent.

This prediction happens *before* your conscious awareness. This is why you can catch a ball without consciously calculating trajectories. This is why you know someone's angry before they say anything.

The prediction system is fast. It's automatic. It works in the background.

Your conscious mind is slow. Your conscious mind is where language happens. Where narrative happens. Where explanation happens.

Here's the problem: Your conscious mind likes to be in control. So when your brain does something, your conscious mind scrambles to create a story about *why* it happened.

And that story becomes your truth.

## The Narrative Machine

There's a famous neuroscience experiment that illustrates this perfectly.

Researchers work with patients who have had their brain split surgically (a rare procedure for severe epilepsy). The left and right hemispheres can't communicate with each other.

The researchers show an instruction to only the right hemisphere: "Pick up that apple."

The patient picks up the apple.

Then the researchers ask the left hemisphere: "Why did you pick up the apple?"

The left hemisphere has no idea. It didn't receive the instruction. But it can't admit that. So it invents a reason.

"Because I was hungry." "Because I like the color." "Because I was curious."

The brain makes up a story. Confidently. Believably. And the person *believes* it.

Here's the thing: Your brain is doing this *all the time*. Not just in bizarre lab conditions. In real life. Every day.

You make a decision. Your conscious mind catches up and creates a narrative about why. You believe that narrative. You act on it. You defend it.

But the narrative isn't the decision. It's the *story you told yourself about* the decision.

## Why This Matters in Corporate Life

You're sitting in a meeting. A colleague suggests something. You immediately feel resistant.

Why? You're not sure. So you do what your brain does: you construct a narrative.

"That won't work because..." "We've already tried that..." "The timeline doesn't allow..."

You sound rational. You sound logical. You sound like you've thought this through.

But here's what actually happened: You *felt* resistant (for reasons you may not even understand). Then your brain generated a plausible narrative to explain the resistance.

Now imagine this happening across a team.

Person A resists an idea. They create a narrative. Person B hears the narrative and creates their own narrative in response. By the end of the meeting, everyone has a different story about what just happened—and they all sound perfectly rational.

You call a follow-up meeting to "align." But people aren't actually sharing different data. They're sharing different narratives. And each narrative makes perfect sense from the inside.

No wonder alignment is so hard.

## The Decision You Don't Control

Here's something else that surprises people: The decision happens *before* you're aware of it.

Neuroscientist Benjamin Libet discovered this in the 1980s. He had people perform simple actions (like pressing a button) while watching a clock. He measured when they reported deciding to act, and when their brain activity showed the decision.

The brain activity showed up *before* the conscious decision.

So the decision was already made. Your conscious mind just reported it after the fact.

Again: Your conscious mind is narrating. It's not deciding.

This doesn't mean you're a puppet. It means decisions are more complex than "I consciously think, therefore I decide."

Instead, it's: Your brain senses context, your unconscious processes it, your brain initiates action, your conscious mind narrates why.

If you're a project manager dealing with a crisis, your instincts kick in. You respond. Your team sees the response. Then later, in a retrospective, you explain *why* you did it.

But the real decision—the pattern recognition that told you something was wrong, the prioritization that decided what to act on first—that happened unconsciously.

Your narrative is the *after-the-fact story*.

## What's Actually Controlling Your Decisions

If it's not your conscious logic, what *is* deciding?

It's your patterns. Your history. Your past experience.

Your brain is constantly pattern-matching. "This situation looks like that situation I dealt with before, so I'll respond similarly."

Most of those patterns are helpful. They're your intuition. Your expertise. Your judgment.

But some of them are outdated. Some of them are incomplete. Some of them are just... wrong.

And because they operate below conscious awareness, you don't notice them. You only notice the narrative your conscious mind creates afterward.

So you think you decided based on logic. But you actually decided based on pattern. And then you narrated it as logic.

## The Confabulation Loop

There's a term for this: **confabulation**.

Confabulation is when your brain fills in gaps in your memory or knowledge with plausible-sounding explanations, without any intent to deceive.

You're not lying. You genuinely believe the story. Your brain constructed it. It makes sense. So you defend it.

In corporate environments, this gets amplified because there's social pressure to have reasons. If someone asks "Why did you decide that?" you can't say "I don't know, it just felt right." You have to provide a narrative.

So your brain does. It generates one. It's convincing. You believe it.

And now you've committed to it. You're defending it. You're making decisions based on it. Because it's the story you told.

Here's an example:

You hire someone. They interview well. You feel good about them. You hire them.

Six months later, they're not working out. You didn't see the warning signs.

But here's what actually happened: There were warning signs. Small things. But you didn't consciously register them because you'd already made the pattern-match decision: "Good hire."

Then, after they fail, you create a narrative: "Nobody could have seen this coming." Or "They changed after we hired them." Or "The role wasn't right."

You're not being dishonest. You're confabulating. You're creating a story that makes sense of why you didn't see what was actually there.

## The Implications for Your Leadership

If decisions happen unconsciously and narratives are created after the fact, what does that mean for you as a leader?

**First: Your instincts are real data. But they're not the whole story.**

Your gut feeling about a situation has value. It's pattern recognition. It's expertise. But it's also limited. It's based on your patterns. Your history. Your incomplete model of the situation.

If you treat your instinct as truth and stop investigating, you're locked into your narrative.

**Second: Other people's narratives are real to them. Even if they're constructed.**

When someone explains why they did something, they're not lying (usually). They're narrating. And their narrative makes sense from inside their perspective.

But it's not necessarily the truth. It's the story their brain created.

This is where so much miscommunication comes from. You each have a different narrative. You each think yours is logical and factual. But you're both narrating.

**Third: The real skill is noticing when you're narrating instead of deciding.**

Once you understand that your conscious mind narrates *after* the decision, you can start to notice it happening.

You feel resistance. Before you construct a narrative justifying it, you can ask: "What am I actually reacting to? What pattern is my brain matching?"

You can separate the decision (which happened unconsciously) from the narrative (which is the story you're telling).

This is awareness. And awareness is where change happens.

## The Observer Model

Here's a useful way to think about it:

You are an observer of your own mind.

Your brain is doing the work. Making the predictions. Processing patterns. Initiating actions.

Your conscious mind is observing that activity and narrating it.

You think you're the actor. But mostly, you're the observer watching the actor and explaining what the actor is doing.

This sounds depressing. But it's actually liberating.

Because if you're an observer, you can notice patterns. You can see when the narrative doesn't match what's actually happening. You can create space between stimulus and response.

And in that space, you can choose differently.

## What This Means for Decision-Making Going Forward

In the next chapters, we're going to talk about how other people work. How groups work. How alignment actually happens.

But first, you need to understand this: **You don't have as much conscious control as you think. But you have more awareness than you're using.**

The skill isn't in controlling your conscious mind better (that's mostly an illusion anyway).

The skill is in understanding what's actually happening underneath. The patterns. The narratives. The unconscious predictions.

Once you understand that, you can lead differently.

You can make decisions faster because you trust your pattern recognition.

You can communicate better because you understand that people's narratives are constructed, not logical.

You can align teams better because you're working with how humans actually work, not how we pretend we work.

## Your First Practice

Before we move to the next chapter, try this:

For one day, notice when you're making a decision or having a reaction. Then notice when you're creating a narrative about it.

You feel irritated at a colleague. You create a narrative: "They're disrespecting me."

You resist a proposal. You create a narrative: "It won't work because..."

You like someone immediately. You create a narrative: "They're really competent."

Don't try to change anything. Just notice.

Notice how fast the narrative comes. How convincing it sounds. How real it feels.

That awareness—that's the beginning of change.

Because once you see the process, you can't unsee it. And once you can't unsee it, you can start to work with it instead of being unconsciously controlled by it.

That's what the rest of this book is about.

# **CHAPTER 2:**  SHARED HEADS

## You Exist Everywhere But Inside Your Own Head

## You Don't Actually Know the People You Work With

Here's something that sounds obvious but changes everything when you really understand it:

When you're interacting with your colleague, you're not actually interacting with them.

You're interacting with your *mental model of them*.

The version of them that lives in your head.

This model is built from: conversations you've had, how they've shown up in meetings, things they've told you, emails they've sent, patterns you've noticed. It's a collection of data points that your brain has organized into a coherent picture.

But it's not them. It's your simulation of them.

And here's the thing: That simulation is incomplete. It's biased. It's shaped by your own filters and patterns.

You might think your colleague is arrogant (because they spoke confidently in a meeting). But in their mind, they were just sharing their expertise. The model you built doesn't match their experience of themselves.

This happens constantly. And it's the source of so much miscommunication.

## The Recursive Mirror

But it gets more complex.

Not only do you have a model of them. They have a model of *you*.

And you're aware of that. So you have a model of their model of you.

And they're aware that you're modeling them. So they have a model of your model of them.

It's recursive. It goes multiple levels deep.

Imagine you're in a performance review with your manager.

You have a model of them: "They're critical. They notice flaws."

They have a model of you: "They're defensive. They don't take feedback well."

You have a model of their model of you: "They think I can't handle critical feedback."

They have a model of your model of their model: "They think I think they're defensive, so they're going to be extra careful what I say."

So in the meeting, you're both carefully managing what you say, anticipating how the other will react, based on your models of each other.

You both leave thinking "that went well" or "that was awkward."

But you might both be reacting to models that don't match reality.

This is the invisible dynamic happening in every interaction.

## The Distributed Self

Here's where it gets really interesting:

**You are not just inside your own head. You exist in everyone else's head too.**

This is what we mean by a distributed self.

There's a version of you that exists in your mind. That's one version.

But there's also a version of you that exists in your manager's mind. A different version (probably). With different attributes and patterns.

There's a version of you in your partner's mind. A version in your team's collective mind. A version in your competitor's mind.

These are all different.

You're not the same person in all these heads. You're a different person in each one, because each person is modeling you based on their limited interactions and their own filters.

Which version is the "real" you?

All of them. None of them.

You're distributed across all of them.

This is why people who work with you describe you differently than you describe yourself. They have a model of you that's not wrong—it's just partial and biased from their perspective.

## Peopling: The Verb That Changes Everything

Now here's the critical insight that most corporate leaders miss:

These distributed versions of you don't maintain themselves. They require maintenance.

That maintenance work is called **peopling**.

Peopling is not a noun. You're not a "person" in the abstract sense. Peopling is what you *do* with other people. It's the action.

It's the conversation. The coffee chat. The one-on-one. The check-in. The joke you make. The story you tell. The way you show up in meetings. The consistency in how you treat people.

All of that is peopling. All of that is maintaining the model other people have of you.

Think about what happens when you have a deep conversation with someone. You share something real. You listen. You ask questions. You create connection.

What's actually happening? You're updating your model of them. They're updating their model of you. You're synchronizing your mental models.

That synchronization is peopling.

## What Happens When Peopling Stops

Here's where it gets practical:

When you stop communicating with someone, what happens to their model of you?

It drifts.

In their mind, you become less updated. More static. More defined by the last thing they remember about you.

This is why relationships drift apart after people leave a company. It's why you can lose touch with old friends. You're not actively maintaining the models anymore.

At work, this shows up as:

You have a one-on-one with your direct report. You connect. You understand each other. The model is synchronized.

Then you're busy for two weeks. No check-ins. No conversation.

When you finally have the next one-on-one, there's friction. Something feels off.

What happened? The models drifted. You each had experiences. You each made assumptions. You each created narratives (remember Chapter 1?) about what's happening with the other.

Now you're out of sync.

The work of peopling is synchronizing those models back up. Updating. Communicating. Sharing context.

## The Split Brain Insight Applied to Teams

Remember the split-brain study from Chapter 1? Where the patient's brain invented narratives?

Teams do the same thing.

You have a project. Person A makes a decision. They create a narrative about why: "This makes sense because of X."

Person B interprets the decision through their own model: "They did this because of Y."

Person C has another interpretation: "It was definitely Z."

None of you are wrong. You're all narrating from your own models.

But your models aren't synchronized. So you're each telling a different story about what's actually happening.

This is why you need meetings. Why you need communication. Why you need people to "get on the same page."

You're not sharing information. You're synchronizing models.

## The Corporate Cost of Asynchronized Models

Let's say you're managing a project. You have a vision for it. You communicate that vision.

But here's what actually happens:

You: "We need to move fast and be bold." Engineer: Models this as "We're going to cut corners and accept technical debt." Designer: Models this as "Minimal viable product, no polish." Product Manager: Models this as "Get something to market before the competitor."

You all heard the same words. But your models of what "bold" means are different.

So as you execute, you're pulling in different directions.

The designer is spending time on polish (inconsistent with the model of bold). The engineer is pushing back on timeline (worried about technical debt). The PM is frustrated that things are taking longer (because their model expected speed).

You call a meeting to "align." Everyone explains their interpretation. You're all shocked because nobody knew the others were modeling it differently.

This happens constantly. And it's invisible until something goes wrong.

The time you waste. The frustration. The rework. The miscommunication. It's all downstream of asynchronized models.

## How Models Influence Who You Become

Here's something else that's profound:

The models people have of you actually shape who you become.

If everyone on your team models you as "the person who handles crises," you'll start to become that person. You'll step into crisis situations. You'll develop those skills. You'll become known for that.

If everyone models you as "the person who's creative," you'll contribute ideas. You'll be invited to brainstorming. You'll develop that part of yourself.

If everyone models you as "not really a leader," you won't be invited into leadership conversations. You won't develop those skills. You'll become that model.

This is why context matters so much. The people around you are literally shaping who you become through the models they hold of you.

This is also why toxic teams are so hard to escape. If everyone models you as "the problem person," it's incredibly hard to become something different because the model is self-reinforcing.

## The Power of the Conversation

Now let's flip this:

If you understand that people hold models of you, and those models shape who you become, then conversations become incredibly powerful.

When you have a real conversation with someone, you're not just exchanging information. You're updating the model they hold of you. You're showing them a different version of yourself.

A conversation where you're vulnerable. Where you admit you don't know something. Where you ask for help.

That conversation updates their model. "Oh, they're more human than I thought. They admit when they don't know."

A conversation where you take responsibility for a mistake. "Oh, they're not defensive. They actually own it."

These conversations, these moments of peopling, are what shift how people see you. What shift who you become.

This is why one-on-ones matter. Why coffee chats matter. Why informal connection matters.

It's not because it's nice to be connected. It's because those interactions are the mechanism by which people update their models of each other.

And updated models are synchronized models. And synchronized models mean better collaboration.

## What You Don't See But Everyone Else Does

Here's an uncomfortable insight:

You have very limited visibility into the model people hold of you. You can guess. You can ask. But you're mostly operating blind.

Meanwhile, you're spending a lot of mental energy on the model they hold of you. You're trying to manage their perception. You're worried about how you come across.

But you're probably wrong about what they actually think.

You think they think you're not decisive. But they might think you're thoughtful.

You think they think you're not a team player. But they might think you have high standards.

The gap between your model of their model of you and their actual model of you is often huge.

This is why people are often shocked by feedback. "Wait, people think *that* about me? I thought they thought something totally different."

You were modeling their model incorrectly.

## The Maintenance Work Most Leaders Skip

Here's where most leaders get it wrong:

They think peopling is optional. A nice-to-have. Something you do when you have time.

But it's not. It's the fundamental infrastructure of collaboration.

When people stop peopling—stop communicating, stop checking in, stop creating connection—models drift. And when models drift, everything gets harder.

Projects take longer because you're constantly clarifying misunderstandings (asynchronized models).

Trust erodes because people are making assumptions (outdated models).

Turnover increases because people feel disconnected (models where they're not seen).

Conflict emerges because people are operating from different narratives (asynchronized models).

The investment in peopling—in conversations, in connection, in synchronization—is the highest-ROI work you can do.

Because everything else depends on it.

## Since COVID, We've Broken the Peopling Rhythm

Here's something that's happened that nobody really talks about:

Since COVID, many organizations lost the informal peopling.

Before: You'd see people in the hallway. You'd chat at lunch. You'd have coffee. Thousands of micro-interactions that were constantly updating models.

Now: You have scheduled meetings. Formal communication. Planned connection.

And model drift is accelerating.

People are more isolated. More in their own heads. Building models of each other with less actual data.

The "meeting fatigue" people talk about? A lot of it is actually the exhaustion of constantly trying to clarify misunderstandings because the informal peopling infrastructure is gone.

The answer isn't fewer meetings. The answer is better peopling. More authentic connection. More real conversation.

But that requires intention. It requires leaders to prioritize it.

## What This Means for Your Leadership

If you understand that:

1. People hold models of you that shape who you become  
2. These models require maintenance through communication  
3. Asynchronized models create friction and slow everything down  
4. Most of the work problems you face are actually model problems, not competence problems

Then your job changes.

Your job isn't just to set direction and execute. Your job is to maintain synchronized models across your team.

To have the conversations that update understanding. To create the space where people see each other clearly. To be intentional about the peopling.

When you do that:

- Decisions get made faster because everyone has the same model of the goal  
- Conflict resolves easier because people understand each other's perspective  
- Teams feel more connected because models are being actively maintained  
- People develop faster because the models people hold of them reflect their potential, not their limitations

## Your Second Practice

For the next week, notice the informal interactions you have with people at work.

The hallway conversation. The Slack message. The five-minute check-in.

These aren't distractions. These are peopling. These are model maintenance.

Notice how you feel after synchronizing—after having a real conversation where you both updated your understanding of each other.

Then notice what happens when that peopling stops for a week. How does the relationship feel different?

This is the mechanism. Once you see it, you can't unsee it.

And once you can't unsee it, you can start to be intentional about it instead of leaving it to chance.

# **CHAPTER 3:**  ALIGNMENT

## Why Most Corporate Work Is Actually Just Synchronization

## The Uncomfortable Truth About Your Meetings

You probably spend about 40% of your week in meetings.

Most of those meetings feel inefficient. People seem distracted. Nobody's really engaged. You could probably "just send an email," right?

Except here's the thing: Those meetings are actually your most important work. You're just not doing them intentionally.

What's actually happening in those meetings is this: **You're synchronizing mental models.**

You're taking everyone's individual, partial understanding of the situation and bringing it into alignment.

That alignment is what allows collaboration to work. Without it, everyone is pulling in different directions based on their incomplete models.

Once you understand this, you stop thinking of meetings as overhead and start thinking of them as infrastructure.

## What Shared Context Actually Is

Shared context isn't information. It's not data.

Shared context is a synchronized understanding of:

- What we're trying to do  
- Why we're doing it  
- What success looks like  
- What constraints we're operating within  
- Who's responsible for what  
- What assumptions we're making

When everyone in a group has roughly the same model of these things, you have shared context.

When people have different models, you don't.

And when you don't have shared context, everything gets harder. Decisions take longer. Miscommunication increases. Rework happens. Friction emerges.

## The Three Types of Synchronization Work

In corporate life, there are basically three things happening:

**Type 1: Status Synchronization** "Here's where things stand. Here's what I've learned. Here's what changed."

This is the project update. The metrics review. The weekly standup.

Why? Because people need to maintain an accurate model of the current state. If your model is outdated, your decisions will be wrong.

**Type 2: Understanding Synchronization** "Here's why I think we should do this. Here's how I understand the problem. Here's what I've noticed."

This is the strategy discussion. The retrospective. The brainstorm.

Why? Because you need to make sure everyone understands not just what's happening, but *why* it's happening. The context. The reasoning.

**Type 3: Alignment Synchronization** "Do we all agree on the direction? On the priorities? On what matters?"

This is the goal-setting meeting. The quarterly planning. The vision conversation.

Why? Because if you're not aligned on what you're actually trying to achieve, everyone will optimize for different things.

Most of what you call "meetings" is actually one of these three types of synchronization work.

And most of the time, it's clunky and inefficient because you're not being intentional about what kind of synchronization you actually need.

## Why Project Failures Are Usually Alignment Failures

You probably know the statistic: Most projects fail not because people lack competence, but because of misalignment.

Think about the last project that went sideways.

What actually happened was: You had different models of what success looked like. Or different models of the constraints. Or different models of what the customer actually needed.

People executed competently. But they executed toward different goals based on different models.

You blamed "poor communication." But really, you just had asynchronized models.

Here's a concrete example:

Project: Build a new feature for your product.

Your model: "We need this to be fast. Get it out in 6 weeks. Iterate based on feedback."

Engineering's model: "We need to build this right. This is foundational infrastructure. It'll take 3 months minimum."

Design's model: "We need this to be beautiful. Let's do research, understand the user, build something elegant."

Sales' model: "We need this now. Customers are asking for it. The longer we wait, the more we lose."

You're all competent. You're all working hard. But you're all executing toward different understandings of what "success" looks like.

So when things don't go as planned, you blame each other. "Engineering is slow. Design is overthinking it. Sales is unrealistic."

But really? You just didn't synchronize your models of the goal.

The fix isn't more meetings. The fix is intentional synchronization. Getting clear on: What are we actually optimizing for? Speed? Quality? Customer satisfaction? Revenue?

Once everyone has the same model, the execution becomes way more coordinated.

## The Real Cost of Misalignment

Let's quantify this:

You have 50 people in your organization. Each with a slightly different model of:

- What the company's strategy is  
- What success looks like  
- What their role contributes to that success  
- What matters most right now

That's 50 people making 1,000 small decisions every day based on 50 different models.

How many of those decisions are working at cross-purposes?

Maybe 20%? 30%?

That's the equivalent of 10-15 people actively working against your goals (not maliciously, just based on different models).

Now imagine you spent time synchronizing those models. Getting everyone to the same understanding.

Suddenly those 50 people are pulling in the same direction.

How much faster do things get done? How much less friction? How much more effective?

This is where the real leverage is.

## How Teams Actually Form and Mature

There's a famous model called "Tuckman's stages of team development": Forming, Storming, Norming, Performing.

Everyone knows about this model. But most people don't understand what's actually happening.

What's actually happening is model synchronization.

**Forming:** People are just meeting each other. Models are incomplete. Everyone's curious. "Who are these people? How do they work?"

**Storming:** People are bumping into each other's different models. "Wait, I thought we were doing X, but they're doing Y." Conflict emerges. People are frustrated. "Why don't they understand?"

**Norming:** Models are starting to synchronize. "Okay, here's how we actually work together. Here's what we've agreed on. Here's how we handle conflict."

**Performing:** Models are well-synchronized. People know each other. They can anticipate. They can collaborate without constant clarification.

The timeline doesn't change if you hire smarter people. It changes if you intentionally synchronize models faster.

A new team that goes through an intensive week of collaboration and conversation will "jell" faster than a team that just assumes they'll figure it out.

Because you're intentionally doing the model synchronization work instead of leaving it to chance.

## The Role of Ritual in Maintaining Alignment

Here's something interesting: The ritualistic aspects of corporate life actually serve a purpose.

The weekly all-hands where you communicate vision. The monthly all-staff where you review metrics. The quarterly planning where you realign.

These aren't just "communication." They're rituals of synchronization.

They maintain the shared context. They keep models from drifting.

When you skip them, models start to drift. People start to interpret things differently. Misalignment creeps in.

This is why remote work has been challenging. Not because people can't do good work remotely. But because you've lost the informal rituals.

The hallway chat where someone updates you on something. The lunch conversation where you share context. The coffee break where you stay connected.

These informal rituals were constantly maintaining shared context. They kept models synchronized without anyone having to plan it.

Now, if you don't intentionally create rituals, models drift.

## The Intimacy-Scale Problem

Here's a constraint of shared context: It gets harder at scale.

With 5 people, shared context is easy. You can have one conversation and be aligned.

With 50 people? Much harder. You need formal meetings. You need documentation. You need intentional communication.

With 500 people? Nearly impossible to have deep shared context across the whole organization.

This is why large organizations often feel fractured. Not because of incompetence. But because shared context is hard to maintain at scale.

This is also why many leaders feel like they're constantly communicating the same message in different ways. "Why do I have to keep saying the vision? Didn't we already do this?"

Because you're trying to maintain shared context across 200 people with 50 different models.

You have to keep repeating it. You have to keep synchronizing.

The alternative is to accept that you can't have deep shared context across the whole organization. Instead, you maintain it within teams. You make sure each team has a strong shared context. And then you synchronize at the team-leader level.

Most organizations get this wrong. They try to maintain deep context across everyone, which is exhausting. Instead, they should accept the constraint and work with it.

## What Breaks Alignment

Alignment breaks when:

**Communication stops.** You stop updating people. Models get stale. People start making assumptions.

**Context changes but isn't communicated.** The environment shifts, but people are still operating from the old model.

**Different groups operate in isolation.** They develop different models and then bump into each other when they try to collaborate.

**Leadership changes without transition.** New leadership brings new models. Old models persist in the organization. Now you have competing models.

**People join and don't get onboarded into the shared context.** They bring their old models from their previous organization.

**You prioritize efficiency over connection.** You cut meetings, skip rituals, assume people will figure it out. Models drift.

## The Meetings That Actually Matter

Not all meetings are equal.

**Meetings that synchronize status:** Useful, but can be async if people actually read the document.

**Meetings that build understanding:** These need to be synchronous. You need dialogue. You need to ask questions and hear reasoning.

**Meetings that align direction:** These definitely need to be synchronous. You need back-and-forth. You need to surface disagreement and resolve it.

Most leaders try to make everything async. But you can't have real dialogue in async. You can't surface disagreement. You can't update models.

The meetings that actually matter are the ones where you're building shared understanding and alignment.

Those need real conversation.

## How to Tell When Alignment Is Breaking

Watch for these signs:

- People making different assumptions about what you're trying to do  
- Decisions taking longer because people keep asking "why are we doing this?"  
- Conflict emerging that feels like it's coming out of nowhere  
- People working on things that seem misaligned with each other  
- New hires asking "wait, I thought we were doing X?"  
- A sense of friction or people feeling unheard

All of these are symptoms of asynchronized models.

The fix isn't to have another meeting about it. The fix is to do the real alignment work. Have the conversation. Surface what people are thinking. Get to actual shared understanding.

Not manufactured agreement. Real shared understanding of what we're doing and why.

## The COVID Disruption and What It Revealed

COVID forced organizations to stop relying on informal rituals for synchronization.

And it revealed something: Many organizations don't have enough intentional synchronization infrastructure.

They were relying on hallway conversations and coffee chats to maintain alignment. Once those went away, alignment broke.

Some organizations adapted. They created new rituals. They were more intentional about communication and synchronization.

Others didn't adapt. They lost alignment. Productivity dropped. Frustration increased.

This is actually an opportunity. Because now we know: Intentional synchronization is better than hoping it happens informally.

It's more reliable. It scales better. It's more equitable (not everyone can network at the coffee machine).

The organizations that figure this out are going to have a competitive advantage.

## What This Means for Your Leadership

If most of your job is synchronizing models, then:

Your job isn't to be the smartest person in the room. It's to make sure everyone has the same understanding of what you're trying to do.

Your job isn't to make all the decisions. It's to make sure people understand the decision and why it was made.

Your job isn't to communicate once. It's to keep communicating until the model is synchronized.

Your job isn't to have more meetings. It's to have the right meetings that actually build shared understanding.

Your job isn't to control people. It's to maintain alignment so people can operate autonomously.

When you get this right, everything gets easier. Decisions happen faster. Collaboration flows. People feel like they understand the direction.

## Your Third Practice

For the next week, notice the actual work that's happening in your meetings.

Not the surface work. The actual work.

What models are being synchronized? What understanding is being built? Where is the alignment breaking or drifting?

Notice how much more efficient meetings become when you're conscious of what you're actually trying to synchronize.

Then notice what happens in the work you do between meetings. How much of it is rework? How much of it is clarification of "wait, I thought we were doing this differently?"

That rework and clarification is the cost of asynchronized models.

Once you see that cost, you understand why the synchronization work actually matters.

# **CHAPTER 4:**  AI AS THE SMART GHOST

## A New Tool for Synchronization (And Why It's Not What You Think)

## The Wrong Question Everyone's Asking

Everyone wants to know: Is AI conscious? Will it become conscious? Should we worry about AI developing its own agenda?

These are fascinating questions. They're also completely missing the point.

The real question isn't whether AI is conscious. The real question is: **Can AI help us do the synchronization work better?**

And the answer is yes. Dramatically yes.

But first, you need to understand what AI actually is. Because most of what you think you know about it is wrong.

## What AI Actually Is

AI isn't magical. It's not mysterious. It's not secretly sentient.

Here's what it actually is:

**AI is a system trained on human language and human knowledge, optimized to predict what text comes next.**

That's it. That's literally what large language models do. They look at text and predict: "What comes next?"

But because they're trained on billions of words of human writing—everything from technical documentation to novels to scientific papers to casual conversation—they've absorbed patterns. Lots of patterns.

These patterns encode human knowledge. Human reasoning. Human problem-solving.

So when you ask an AI a question, it's not consulting a database. It's pattern-matching against all the language it's seen. Drawing on patterns it recognizes. Generating a response that has the statistical properties of human-written text addressing that kind of question.

It's sophisticated pattern matching. But it's not magic. And it's not consciousness.

## Why AI Seems Intelligent

Here's something that will blow your mind:

The reason AI seems intelligent is not because the AI is smart. It's because *human culture* is smart.

All the intelligence you see in AI comes from humanity. Centuries of human thought. Human problem-solving. Human discovery. All encoded in language.

When an AI writes code, it's not inventing programming. It's recognizing patterns in millions of lines of code written by humans. It's pattern-matching against what humans have already figured out.

When an AI helps with writing, it's not creating new ideas. It's recognizing patterns in good writing. It's pattern-matching against quality examples.

When an AI solves a problem, it's not thinking in some alien way. It's recognizing patterns similar to how humans have solved similar problems.

The intelligence is in our culture. In our language. In our accumulated knowledge.

AI is like a mirror that reflects back the intelligence of humanity. A very sophisticated mirror. But a mirror nonetheless.

This is why an AI trained on internet data is different from an AI trained on technical documentation. It's not that one is smarter. It's that they've absorbed different patterns from different parts of human knowledge.

## The "Smart Ghost" Concept

Think of AI as a smart ghost.

A ghost in the sense that it's not a person. It has no interiority. No inner experience. No consciousness (almost certainly).

But it's smart in the sense that it has absorbed patterns from billions of words of human intelligence.

It can engage in conversation. It can solve problems. It can generate ideas. It can help you think through things.

But it's not thinking in the way you think. It's not conscious in the way you're conscious.

It's a very sophisticated pattern-matching system that has absorbed enough of human intelligence to be genuinely useful.

And once you understand that, you can stop worrying about whether it's conscious or developing an agenda, and start thinking about: How can I use this tool?

## The Consciousness Question (And Why It's the Wrong One)

Let's address this directly because it's what everyone's worried about:

Is AI conscious? Will it become conscious?

Honestly? We don't even know what consciousness is in humans. We can't measure it. We can't define it precisely.

Some people think animals aren't conscious. Some people think they are. Some people think only mammals are. Some people think only humans are.

The question is genuinely hard.

But here's the thing: It doesn't matter for what we're trying to do.

Even if an AI isn't conscious, it can still help you. It can still accelerate your work. It can still improve your collaboration.

A calculator isn't conscious, but it helps you with math. A map isn't conscious, but it helps you navigate. An AI isn't conscious, but it can help you with thinking and communication.

The question "Is it conscious?" is interesting philosophically. But it's a distraction from the practical question: "Can it help me do better work?"

And the answer to that is clearly yes.

## What AI Can Actually Do (And What It Can't)

AI is genuinely useful for:

- **Summarization**: Taking complex information and making it concise  
- **Translation**: Converting ideas from one form to another (technical to plain language, written to visual, etc.)  
- **Draft generation**: Creating first drafts that you then refine  
- **Pattern recognition**: Spotting patterns in data or text  
- **Exploration**: Helping you think through different angles of a problem  
- **Clarification**: Helping you articulate something you're struggling to express

AI is not useful for:

- **Making decisions for you**: Because it doesn't understand your values or constraints  
- **Building trust**: Because people trust people, not systems  
- **Creating understanding**: Real understanding requires dialogue, not just information  
- **True creativity**: It can remix patterns, but it can't actually think of something genuinely new

Here's the pattern: AI is useful for the mechanical parts of thinking. The pattern-matching. The articulation. The translation.

AI is not useful for the human parts of thinking. The value judgment. The relationships. The real creativity.

## How AI Becomes Relevant to Your Synchronization Problem

Here's where this connects back to Chapters 1-3:

The synchronization work you do (the peopling, the alignment, the model synchronization) is mostly about translation.

You have one person's understanding and you need to translate it for another person's model. You need to take a complex idea and simplify it. You need to take a technical concept and make it accessible.

This is where AI actually shines.

An AI can help you articulate something you're struggling to express.

An AI can help translate a technical decision into business language, so the business side understands it.

An AI can help take a vision statement and generate concrete examples, so people understand what you actually mean.

An AI can help take feedback and reframe it in a way that lands better.

In other words: **AI can help you do the context bridging work faster and better.**

And context bridging is what accelerates synchronization.

## The AI Agent as a Collaborative Partner

Here's where it gets interesting:

You can start thinking of AI as a collaborative partner. Not in the sense of it being a person. But in the sense of it being useful in collaboration.

And when you do that, you can apply some of the same principles we've been discussing.

You can have a model of how the AI works. What it's good at. What it's bad at. How to communicate with it to get the best results.

You can understand that the AI has patterns and limitations, just like humans have patterns and limitations.

You can build context with the AI—giving it information about your situation, your values, your constraints.

You can use the AI as a thinking partner to help you articulate and refine your ideas.

In a sense, the AI becomes a tool for synchronization. Not just with other people, but for your own thinking.

## The Real Revolution: Context Bridging at Scale

Here's what actually changes with AI:

In the past, if you wanted to translate a concept for different audiences, you needed a person to do it. Different people for different translations.

Now, an AI can do context bridging at scale.

Imagine you have a strategy document. You want different versions for different audiences:

- For the engineering team (technical, focused on implementation)  
- For the sales team (business-focused, focused on customer impact)  
- For new hires (foundational, context-setting)  
- For the board (financial, outcome-focused)

In the past, you'd need to write four different documents. Or four meetings. Or four different conversations.

Now, an AI can help you generate these tailored versions automatically.

Or imagine you're trying to build alignment across 500 people. You can't have one big meeting. But you can create tailored communication for different groups, all rooted in the same core understanding.

This is what changes: **The speed at which you can do context bridging scales.**

And because context bridging is what accelerates synchronization, this means synchronization itself can happen faster.

## The Integration Point: AI \+ Peopling

Here's how AI actually becomes useful in the framework we've been building:

You do the real peopling work. The relationships. The conversations. The building of trust.

You create shared understanding through dialogue and connection.

Then you use AI to scale that understanding. To translate it. To adapt it. To help communicate it to different audiences.

The AI doesn't replace the peopling. But it amplifies it.

You have a deep conversation with your leadership team. You build real understanding. You figure out the strategy.

Then you use AI to help translate that strategy into communications for different parts of the organization.

You work with your team on building a shared model. You have the conversations. You get to understanding.

Then you use AI to help document that understanding. To articulate it. To make it accessible.

You're not replacing the human work. You're using AI to scale the human work.

## What This Means for Teams

When AI is used this way—as a tool for context bridging and synchronization—teams actually move faster.

Not because people are working harder. But because the synchronization work (which is most of the work) becomes more efficient.

You spend less time in meetings trying to explain things. Because AI can help you articulate and clarify more efficiently.

You spend less time on miscommunication. Because you can create tailored communication for different groups, ensuring better alignment.

You spend less time on rework. Because synchronization happens faster, so people are working toward aligned models.

It's not magic. It's just applying a new tool to the existing challenge of synchronization.

## The Risk: Using AI to Replace Peopling

Here's where it goes wrong:

Some organizations try to use AI to *replace* peopling. To use AI-generated communication instead of real conversation. To skip the relationship-building and just rely on AI summaries.

This doesn't work. Because synchronization requires dialogue. It requires real understanding. It requires relationship.

An AI can help translate. But it can't create trust. It can't build understanding in the way that real conversation does.

If you try to use AI to avoid the peopling work, you'll end up with asynchronized models. Faster asynchronization, maybe, but asynchronization nonetheless.

The trick is to use AI to amplify the peopling, not replace it.

## How to Think About AI Going Forward

Here's the mental model to hold:

AI is a tool. A powerful tool. But a tool.

It's good at pattern matching and translation. It's bad at values judgment and relationships.

Use it for what it's good at. Don't try to make it do what it's bad at.

It's not conscious (probably). So don't worry about it developing an agenda. And don't treat it like it's a person.

It's not going to replace human work. But it will change how human work gets done.

It's amplifying human capability, not replacing it.

The organizations that figure out how to use AI for context bridging and synchronization are going to move faster. Because they'll have faster alignment. Because they'll do the peopling work more efficiently.

But they'll still have to do the peopling work.

## Your Fourth Practice

Spend some time thinking about where context bridging happens in your organization.

Where do ideas need to be translated? Where do people speak different languages (technical vs. business, etc.)? Where does miscommunication happen because something's not being understood?

These are the places where AI can help.

Think about how you could use AI to speed up context bridging in these places.

Not to replace the conversation. But to amplify it. To make the translation faster. To help people understand each other better.

Once you start seeing AI this way—not as a replacement for human work, but as a tool for accelerating human synchronization—you stop worrying about whether it's conscious and start thinking about how to use it.

# **CHAPTER 5:**  SOLVING FOR YOU

## Finding Meaning and Establishing Coherence

## The Fractured Self

You're not the same person at work that you are at home.

That's obvious. Everyone code-switches. We adjust based on context.

But here's what's not obvious: The degree of mismatch between your different selves has a massive impact on your effectiveness and well-being.

Some people are relatively integrated. They're roughly the same person across different contexts. Their values are consistent. Their behavior is consistent. The model others have of them is pretty aligned with their actual self.

Other people are extremely fragmented. The version of them at work is dramatically different from the version at home. They wear a mask. They perform. They manage their image.

The fragmentation creates stress. It creates friction. It creates the sense that people don't actually know you.

And it makes leadership harder.

## The Cost of Fragmentation

Let's think about what happens when you're highly fragmented:

At work, you're the "professional." Buttoned-up. Always composed. Never vulnerable. Always knowing the answer.

At home, you're different. More relaxed. More honest. More yourself.

The energy cost of maintaining that divide is significant. You're constantly monitoring yourself. Am I acting right for this context? Am I showing the right version? Am I saying the right thing?

This is what we mean by low agency. You're not acting from a place of authenticity. You're performing. You're worried about how you come across.

The other cost: People at work don't actually know you. They know the professional persona. They build a model of you based on that persona. But it's not the real you.

So when you finally show a bit of authenticity—you make a joke, you admit you don't know something, you show emotion—people are surprised. "I didn't know you were like that."

They're reacting to the fact that their model of you was incomplete.

## High Agency vs Low Agency

There's a spectrum here:

**Low Agency:** You're constantly worried about how you're perceived. You're performing. You're managing your image. You choose your words carefully. You avoid showing weakness. You're stressed about being "found out."

**High Agency:** You're comfortable being yourself. You're not trying to manage perception. You say what you think. You admit when you don't know. You're relaxed about being misunderstood because you're not trying to control the narrative.

High agency people are actually easier to trust. Paradoxically.

Because they're not trying to manipulate perception, people trust their authenticity. They know what they're getting.

Low agency people, even if they're not trying to deceive anyone, create suspicion. Because people sense the performance. They sense the gap between the persona and the actual person.

This is why authentic leaders are often more trusted than "perfect" leaders. They're modeling integration.

## What Coherence Actually Means

Coherence (or congruence) doesn't mean you're the same in every context. It means your core values and sense of self are consistent across contexts.

You might adjust your expression based on context. You might be more formal in some settings and more casual in others.

But underneath, you're roughly the same person. Your values are consistent. Your integrity is consistent. People can predict how you'll respond because you're not fundamentally different.

This is actually harder to achieve than people think. Because there are real contextual demands.

The workplace often demands professionalism, which gets interpreted as "hide your humanity." Family life demands vulnerability and connection. Friendships demand humor and informality.

The temptation is to compartmentalize. To be completely different in each context.

But true coherence is different. It's being approximately yourself across contexts while adjusting your expression based on what's appropriate.

The difference is subtle but significant.

## How Your Model of Yourself Shapes Your Behavior

Remember from Chapter 1: Your brain works by pattern. Your decisions are largely unconscious. Your conscious mind narrates.

The same applies to your model of yourself.

If your model of yourself is "I'm not a public speaker," then you'll avoid speaking opportunities. Your brain will find reasons. "I'm too busy. I don't know enough. Someone else should do it."

If your model of yourself is "I'm organized," then you'll maintain organization. Your habits will support it. You'll be the person who makes lists.

If your model of yourself is "I'm not creative," then you'll avoid creative work. You'll assume others are more suited for it.

Your model of yourself becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy.

The good news: You can change your model of yourself.

It takes time. It requires consistent evidence that contradicts the old model. But it's possible.

If you take on a speaking opportunity and do okay, that contradicts the "not a speaker" model. You do another. Gradually, the model shifts.

## The Fragmentation at Work Problem

Here's where it gets relevant to your leadership:

Most people feel pressure to fragment at work. To hide the parts of themselves that aren't "professional."

They hide:

- Uncertainty (always projecting confidence)  
- Emotion (always composed)  
- Humanity (always buttoned-up)  
- Values (anything outside work is irrelevant)  
- Interests (anything that doesn't support the professional image)

The organization creates this pressure (intentionally or not) by rewarding the professional persona and punishing authenticity.

"We need people who are always professional." "We value composed leaders." "Don't bring your personal stuff to work."

And people respond by fragmenting.

But here's what happens: If everyone is fragmenting, then no one really knows anyone else. Everyone is building models based on incomplete information.

And as we learned in Chapter 3, asynchronized models create friction.

People are confused about each other's motivations. They're making assumptions. They're creating narratives about why someone did something, when really they just misunderstood because they only know the professional persona.

## The Vulnerability Paradox

There's something counterintuitive here:

When leaders show authentic humanity—admit uncertainty, share struggles, show emotion appropriately—people actually trust them more.

Not less. More.

Because authenticity signals that you're not trying to manipulate or control. You're not performing.

This is the vulnerability paradox: The more you try to control perception (by performing), the less trustworthy you become. The more you allow your authentic self to show, the more trustworthy you become.

Of course, there are boundaries. There's a difference between appropriate vulnerability (admitting you don't have all the answers) and inappropriate vulnerability (sharing your divorce details with your direct reports).

But within those boundaries, authenticity builds trust faster than performance ever will.

## What Changes When You Become More Coherent

When you stop fragmenting and become more integrated:

**Decision-Making Gets Clearer**

Because you're not constantly filtering your decisions through "what should I do?" and "what should I be seen as doing?" You're just operating from your actual values.

Decisions are faster. They feel more authentic. And they're more defensible because they're rooted in your real values, not a performed image.

**Leadership Gets Easier**

Because people can actually understand you. Your model is less fragmented. So they predict your behavior better. They understand your values. They trust your decisions.

**Relationships Deepen**

Because people know the actual you, not the persona. There's real connection instead of professional courtesy.

**Stress Decreases**

Because you stop spending energy maintaining the facade. You're just being yourself.

## The Identity Upgrade

Here's a way to think about this:

Your identity is your model of yourself. "I'm a leader." "I'm not technical." "I'm creative." "I'm organized."

These models shape your behavior and your choices.

If you want to change your behavior, you often need to change your model of yourself first.

This is what we mean by "identity upgrade."

It's not about willpower. It's not about trying harder. It's about updating your fundamental model of who you are.

Examples:

You want to be more confident. But your model is "I'm anxious." Your confidence will be limited because your model will pull you back.

The upgrade: Develop evidence that you're someone who can be calm. Small wins. Situations where you stay composed. Gradually update the model: "I'm someone who can manage my nerves."

You want to be a public speaker. But your model is "I'm not a public speaker." You'll avoid opportunities.

The upgrade: Give a small talk. Do it imperfectly. But do it. Update the model: "I'm someone learning to speak publicly."

You want to be a leader. But your model is "I'm an individual contributor." You'll struggle.

The upgrade: Take on leadership. Make leadership decisions. Get feedback. Update the model: "I'm a leader."

## The Meaning Piece

There's another aspect to "solving for you": Understanding what actually matters to you.

Most people never ask this question seriously. They just inherit goals from society or their organization.

"I should make good money." "I should advance to a leadership position." "I should be successful."

These are fine. But are they *your* goals? Or are they goals you've inherited?

The people who are most effective and fulfilled have usually done the work of figuring out what actually matters to them. Not what they think should matter. What actually matters.

Maybe it's:

- Creating something meaningful  
- Building strong relationships  
- Solving important problems  
- Growing and learning  
- Having autonomy  
- Contributing to a cause  
- Achieving a specific result

The specifics vary. But the clarity is important.

Because once you know what actually matters to you, your decision-making becomes clearer. Your priorities become clearer. Your model of yourself becomes more coherent.

If you know that autonomy matters to you, then you'll make choices that protect autonomy. You'll avoid situations where you're micromanaged, even if they pay more.

If you know that relationships matter, then you'll invest in connection. You'll choose teams over solo work.

If you know that growth matters, then you'll seek challenges. You'll move toward difficulty instead of away from it.

## Your Fifth Practice: The Identity Audit

Take some time with these questions:

# **CHAPTER 6:**  SOLVING FOR CORPORATION

## Building Trust, Alignment, and Fast-Tracked Progress

## The Coherent Organization

Most organizations are fragmented. Different departments speak different languages. People in different parts of the company have different models of what the company is trying to do. Leadership says one thing; the culture shows another.

People come to work in different versions of themselves. The engineer is in "technical mode." The salesperson is in "pitch mode." The accountant is in "numbers mode."

Everyone's performing. Everyone's managing their image.

And the organization pays the price: Slow decisions. Miscommunication. Rework. Friction. Burned-out people.

A coherent organization is different.

A coherent organization has a shared understanding of what it's trying to do and why. Leadership is authentic. People can be themselves. There's trust.

And these organizations move fast. They execute well. They retain people.

The difference isn't competence. It's coherence.

## How Leadership Incoherence Spreads

Here's how it works:

You're a leader. You're fragmented. You present a perfect image at the top. You hide your uncertainty. You present your decisions as if they were logical conclusions, even when you made them based on intuition.

Your team sees this. They model you. They think: "This is what leadership looks like. You always have the answer. You never show weakness. You never admit uncertainty."

So they fragment too. They show you the professional version. They hide their uncertainty. They pretend to have it figured out.

Now you're leading a team of people who are fragmenting. They're not bringing their full selves. They're not collaborating authentically. They're performing.

And this cascades. Their direct reports see the performance. They fragment too.

Within a few levels, you have an organization where everyone is performing. Everyone is managing their image. Nobody is being authentic.

And the cost is massive. Decision-making is slow because people are afraid to voice uncertainty. Collaboration is stifled because people are protecting their image. Trust is low because people sense the performance.

## How Leadership Coherence Cascades

But the opposite is also true:

You're a leader. You've done the work on yourself. You have a clear model of yourself. You know your values. You're coherent.

You show up authentically. You admit when you don't know something. You share your reasoning, including the intuitive parts. You show appropriate emotion.

Your team sees this. They model it. They think: "This is what leadership looks like. You can be authentic. You can admit uncertainty. You can be real."

So they become more authentic too. They speak up with ideas. They admit when they don't understand. They ask for help.

Now you're leading a team of people who are being authentic. Who are collaborating genuinely. Who trust each other.

And this cascades. Their direct reports see the authenticity. They become more authentic.

Within a few levels, you have an organization where people are being themselves. Where trust is high. Where collaboration is genuine.

And the benefits are massive. Decision-making is fast because people voice concerns early. Collaboration is rich because people are bringing their full selves. Trust is high because people know what they're getting.

## Trust at Organizational Scale

Trust is the currency of fast-moving organizations.

When there's high trust:

- People give you the benefit of the doubt  
- Miscommunications get resolved quickly instead of creating drama  
- People step up without being asked  
- People stay longer  
- Collaboration flows  
- Decisions happen faster

When there's low trust:

- Everything takes longer because people are protecting themselves  
- Miscommunications spiral  
- People need explicit permission and monitoring  
- Turnover is high  
- Collaboration is stifled  
- Decisions get stuck in review cycles

The question is: How do you build trust at scale?

It starts with coherent leadership. A leader who is authentic creates an environment where people feel safe being authentic.

But it requires more than just the leader. It requires practices and rituals that reinforce psychological safety across the organization.

## Psychological Safety and Model Accuracy

Here's the connection to what we've been discussing:

Psychological safety is the belief that you can take interpersonal risks (being yourself, admitting mistakes, asking for help) without being punished or humiliated.

When you have psychological safety, you can be authentic. You can share your actual model of things instead of performing.

And when people share their actual models, everyone's models become more accurate.

You're not building models based on performed versions. You're building models based on the actual person.

This is why psychologically safe teams are more effective. It's not just about feelings. It's about model accuracy.

When everyone's models of each other are accurate, collaboration becomes more efficient. Miscommunication decreases. Trust increases.

The leader's job is to create conditions where this is possible.

## The Three Practices of Coherent Organizations

There are three main practices that create coherent organizations:

**Practice 1: Clear, Repeated Communication of What Matters**

You need to repeatedly communicate:

- What the organization is trying to do  
- Why it matters  
- How people's work contributes to that  
- What success looks like

Not once. Repeatedly. In different formats. From different leaders.

Because people's models of "what we're doing" drift. You have to keep synchronizing.

The organizations that do this well have a communication rhythm: Weekly all-hands. Monthly all-staff. Quarterly reviews.

Not because they're paranoid about communication. But because they understand that synchronization requires repetition.

**Practice 2: Authentic Leadership**

Leaders need to be coherent. Authentic. Willing to be vulnerable appropriately.

This doesn't mean oversharing. It doesn't mean being unprofessional.

It means:

- Admitting what you don't know  
- Sharing your reasoning (including intuitive parts)  
- Acknowledging mistakes  
- Showing genuine emotion  
- Being consistent (same person across contexts)

When leaders do this, it creates permission for everyone else to be authentic.

**Practice 3: Rituals of Connection**

Organizations need formal and informal rituals that maintain peopling.

Formal rituals:

- One-on-ones with your direct reports (not checking in on work, actually connecting)  
- Team meetings that include space for non-work connection  
- All-hands where people hear the vision and values  
- Off-sites where people get to know each other more deeply

Informal rituals:

- Spaces where people naturally encounter each other  
- Slack channels for off-topic conversation  
- Coffee culture (whether physical or virtual)  
- Celebrating wins together

These rituals are what maintain the models people have of each other. They're what keep the organization from fragmenting.

## The Communication Rhythm That Works

Most organizations have random communication. Leadership communicates when they remember. Or when something's urgent.

But random communication creates drift. Models become asynchronized.

Coherent organizations have a predictable communication rhythm:

**Daily:** Team standups (sync status, maintain connection)

**Weekly:** All-hands or team meeting (company-wide updates, vision reinforcement)

**Bi-weekly or Monthly:** All-staff meeting or company event (deeper alignment, celebration, connection)

**Quarterly:** All-hands \+ strategy review (realignment, vision refresh)

**Annually:** Offsite or retreat (deep connection, strategy setting)

This rhythm isn't arbitrary. It's based on how long it takes for models to drift.

Daily standup: Prevents status drift (your understanding of what people are working on) Weekly all-hands: Prevents strategy drift (your understanding of what we're doing) Monthly all-staff: Prevents cultural drift (your understanding of who we are) Quarterly review: Prevents vision drift (your understanding of where we're going)

Miss the rhythm, and drift accelerates.

## The Role of Transparency

Coherent organizations are transparent. Not about everything. But about what matters.

Transparent about:

- Business performance (revenue, margins, growth)  
- Strategic direction (where we're going, why)  
- Challenges (what we're struggling with)  
- Decision-making (why we decided what we did)

Transparency doesn't mean oversharing. It means sharing what helps people understand.

When people understand the business, they can make better decisions. When they know the challenges, they can help solve them. When they know the strategy, they can align their work.

Transparency also signals trust. When leadership is transparent, people trust that they're being told the truth. When leadership is opaque, people assume the worst.

## What Breaks Organizational Coherence

Coherence breaks when:

**Leadership changes without transition.** New leadership brings new models. Old leadership's models persist. Confusion.

**Communication stops.** Leadership gets busy. Communication gets sporadic. Models drift.

**There's a gap between what's said and what's done.** Leadership says "we value collaboration" but promotes the person who stepped on people to get ahead. Now nobody believes what leadership says.

**Different leaders give different messages.** One leader says "move fast and break things." Another says "quality over speed." People are confused about what actually matters.

**The organization grows without intentionality.** You go from 20 people (where everyone knows everyone) to 200 people and keep communicating the same way. Models drift.

**Remote work happens without adapting rituals.** You lose the informal peopling and don't create intentional rituals to replace it. Models drift.

**New people join without proper onboarding.** They bring models from their last organization. They don't absorb the culture. Models drift.

## The Onboarding Lens

Here's a useful way to think about organizational coherence: Your onboarding process.

If your onboarding is good, new people quickly absorb:

- What the organization is trying to do  
- How it actually works (not how it's supposed to work)  
- What actually matters  
- What the values are in practice (not just in posters)

If your onboarding is poor, new people never really understand. They operate from assumptions and models from their previous organization.

A good onboarding has:

- Clear explanation of what the organization does and why it matters  
- Introduction to key people and how they actually operate  
- Stories of how decisions are made  
- Real examples of what success looks like  
- Time to actually ask questions and get to know people

The best onboarding isn't formal. It's just: You spend time with people who can explain the actual culture.

## Team-Level Coherence

You can create coherence at the team level even if the organization isn't fully coherent.

A team with:

- Clear, shared understanding of what you're trying to do  
- A leader who's authentic  
- Regular communication and connection  
- Psychological safety

This team will move fast. They'll collaborate well. They'll retain people.

Even if the broader organization is fragmented.

This is why good managers create culture within their teams. They're creating local coherence.

And this is actually a path to organizational coherence. Good teams create good culture. Good culture spreads. Over time, the organization becomes more coherent.

## The Speed of Coherence

Here's the bottom line:

A coherent organization moves dramatically faster than a fragmented one.

Not because people are smarter. Not because they work harder.

But because they're not spending time managing perception. They're not dealing with miscommunication. They're not doing rework because models were asynchronized.

They're just working toward a shared goal with accurate models of each other.

The best organizations in the world—the ones that move fast and retain talent—are coherent organizations.

They've figured out how to build trust. How to align around what matters. How to create space for people to be themselves.

And these aren't organizations with perfect harmony. They still have disagreement. They still have conflict.

But the disagreement is about substance. Not about misunderstanding. The conflict is productive. Not destructive.

Because the models are accurate.

# **CHAPTER 7:**  AI SHARED CONTEXT

## Context Bridging and Reading the Room

## The Real Work: Translation and Tailoring

You have a strategy. It's clear in your head. You know what you're trying to do and why.

But you need to communicate it to:

- Engineers (who think in systems and constraints)  
- Sales (who think in customer problems and revenue)  
- Finance (who think in numbers and risk)  
- New people (who don't have the context yet)  
- Your board (who need the 30,000-foot view)  
- Investors (who need the growth story)

Each group needs a different translation of the same strategy. Different language. Different emphasis. Different examples.

In the past, you had to manually write these different versions. Or have different meetings. Or hope people could translate for themselves.

Now, AI can help. It can help you translate your core strategy into the language of each group.

And that translation work—that context bridging—is what synchronization actually is.

## Reading the Room

Before you can bridge context, you need to read the room.

Reading the room means understanding:

- What does this person/group already know?  
- What's their model of the situation right now?  
- What language do they speak?  
- What concerns them?  
- What will actually land with them?

In-person, you do this through observation. You watch faces. You notice energy. You sense when people are confused or skeptical.

Over Zoom, you lose some of that. Text-based, you lose even more.

But you can still do it. You pay attention to questions people ask. You notice what they focus on. You listen for what's unsaid.

Then you adjust. You don't use technical language with non-technical people. You don't lead with revenue with engineers. You don't lead with strategy with people asking operational questions.

This is context bridging. You're translating the same core thing into the language and concerns of your audience.

AI can help with this. It can't actually read the room (it's not there). But it can help you translate your message for different audiences based on what you tell it about them.

## Practical Use Case 1: Strategy Translation

You have a company strategy: "Shift our product from single-user to team-based collaboration."

You need to communicate this to different groups:

**For Engineering:** You want to emphasize:

- Architecture changes required  
- Timeline and phases  
- Technical challenges and solutions  
- How this impacts existing code

**For Sales:** You want to emphasize:

- New market opportunities  
- How to position the feature  
- Competitive advantages  
- Customer pain points it solves

**For Finance:** You want to emphasize:

- Investment required  
- Expected revenue impact  
- Timeline to profitability  
- Risk and mitigation

**For New Hires:** You want to emphasize:

- Why we're doing this  
- How their role contributes  
- What success looks like  
- How it connects to our mission

Manually writing four different documents is time-consuming.

With AI, you write one core strategy document. Then you prompt the AI:

"Take this strategy and write a version for our engineering team, emphasizing architecture and timeline."

"Take this strategy and write a version for sales, focusing on market opportunity and customer positioning."

And so on.

The AI generates translations that are tailored to each audience. Each one is accurate to the core strategy. But each one speaks the language of that group.

Now you spend 30 minutes generating all four versions instead of three hours writing them manually.

## Practical Use Case 2: Feedback Translation

You have to give feedback to a team member. It's critical feedback.

Your feedback: "You interrupted people in the meeting. You seemed frustrated. It undermined trust."

But how does that land with different people?

Some people are defensive. They hear criticism and shut down. You need to lead with empathy and intent clarity: "I noticed something I want to talk about because I care about your growth."

Some people are analytical. They want context and data. You need to be specific: "In the meeting at 2:15 PM, you interrupted Sarah three times. I noticed your tone shifted when..."

Some people are sensitive to relationships. They hear feedback as rejection. You need to lead with relationship: "I want to be straight with you because I respect you and our relationship."

AI can help you translate the same feedback into different styles.

You write the core feedback. Then:

"Rewrite this for someone who responds well to empathy and intent clarity."

"Rewrite this for someone who wants specific data and examples."

"Rewrite this for someone who is sensitive to relationship implications."

The AI generates versions that are all honest and accurate. But each one is tailored to how that person receives feedback.

## Practical Use Case 3: Problem Translation

You have a problem to solve. Your product latency is too high.

This is a problem for:

- Engineering (technical challenge to solve)  
- Product (feature velocity impact)  
- Sales (customer experience impact)  
- Finance (infrastructure cost impact)

Each group needs to understand the problem, but from their angle:

**For Engineering:** The problem is about architecture and performance optimization.

**For Product:** The problem is about competitive positioning and feature roadmap impact.

**For Sales:** The problem is about customer experience and competitive disadvantage.

**For Finance:** The problem is about infrastructure cost and investment ROI.

Same problem. Four different translations.

AI can help. You describe the problem once. The AI translates it for different audiences.

## Practical Use Case 4: Decision Communication

You made a decision. You want people to understand why.

Your decision: "We're shifting from outsourced support to in-house support."

But different people care about different aspects:

**For the support team:** They care about their role and job security.

**For customers:** They care about service quality and responsiveness.

**For finance:** They care about cost structure and profitability.

**For product:** They care about customer feedback and how it influences product.

You need to communicate the same decision, but emphasizing different benefits for different groups.

AI can help you generate these tailored communications quickly.

## How to Use AI for Context Bridging

Here's the workflow:

**Step 1: Get Clear on Your Core Message**

Write or speak your core message. What do you actually want people to understand?

Don't worry about making it perfect. Just get it out.

**Step 2: Identify Your Audiences**

Who needs to understand this? What does each group care about? What's their current model?

**Step 3: Prompt the AI**

"Take this message and tailor it for \[audience\]. Emphasize \[what matters to them\]. They currently believe \[their model\]. Help them understand \[what you want them to get\]. Speak their language."

**Step 4: Refine**

The AI generates a draft. You review it. Does it capture the essence? Does it sound authentic? Does it hit the right notes for that audience?

Refine if needed. Then use it.

**Step 5: Send/Share**

You now have tailored communications for each group. You can share them in one-on-ones, meetings, emails, etc.

## The Right Way to Use This

The key insight: You're not letting the AI decide for you. You're using it to amplify your work.

You decide what matters. You decide the core message. You decide what each audience needs.

The AI handles the translation labor.

This is fundamentally different from asking AI to make the decision. You're asking it to translate your decision.

This maintains authenticity. Each communication is still coming from you. Your actual values. Your actual reasoning.

The AI is just making the translation work faster.

## Practical Use Case 5: Sensing What's Unsaid

This one is subtler.

You're in a meeting. Someone asks a question that seems tangential to the topic.

You could dismiss it. Or you could recognize it as a signal that they don't understand something. That their model is incomplete.

You could prompt an AI (in your head, not literally):

"This person asked about X. That suggests they might be worried about Y (even though they didn't say it). What could I say to address the underlying concern?"

Or literally, you could step out of the meeting, jot down the question, prompt an AI:

"In a meeting about \[topic\], someone asked \[question\]. What concern might be underlying this? How could I address it?"

The AI might surface: "They might be worried about how this impacts their team's stability. You could address that by being clear about what's not changing."

This helps you read the room better. It helps you sense what's unsaid.

## Reading the Room: The Emotional Component

There's one thing AI can't do: Actually perceive the emotional state in the room.

But you can. You can see faces. You can hear tone. You can sense energy.

The skill is combining what you perceive with AI's help generating responses.

You sense the room is skeptical. You could:

1. Notice: "People seem skeptical. I need to address this differently."  
2. Prompt AI: "People seem skeptical about this decision. What concerns might be driving that? How could I address it?"  
3. Adjust: You incorporate the AI's insights into your response. You address the underlying concerns.

You're combining your human perception with AI's ability to help you think through implications and responses.

## When NOT to Use AI for Context Bridging

There are limits. Important ones.

**Don't use AI when you should have the conversation yourself.**

If you're avoiding difficult feedback, don't use AI to generate a softened version. Have the conversation. Be authentic.

If you're trying to hide something, don't use AI to make it sound better. That's manipulation.

**Don't use AI as a substitute for knowing your audience.**

The AI can help translate. But it works better when you actually know what your audience cares about.

If you don't know, the translation won't be as good.

**Don't use AI for big decisions that require your judgment.**

The AI can help you think through angles. But decisions about direction, values, big moves—these need your judgment.

**Don't use AI to replace real relationship building.**

The best communication still comes from real relationship. From actually knowing people. From real dialogue.

AI can supplement. But it can't replace.

## The Trust Component

Here's something important:

You can use AI to translate your communication. But the trust still comes from you.

If you have a good relationship with someone, they'll trust your AI-assisted communication because they trust you.

If you have a poor relationship, even well-written AI-translated communication won't build trust.

The AI makes good communication better. It doesn't create trust from nothing.

This is why the coherence work from Chapter 6 matters. The organizational trust allows AI-assisted communication to be effective.

### The Three Questions to Ask Yourself

Fixing it is often simpler than you think.
