# Setting the Scene

*Foreword. The problem before any of the solution. If you don't recognise yourself somewhere in here, this probably isn't your book. If you recognise yourself in all four, keep reading.*

---

It's a Tuesday and you're already tired, and you haven't done anything yet.

You woke up at three in the morning running the same loop you went to sleep on: how to get Johnny and Jimmy and the rest of them pointing the same direction by Thursday. Not because they're bad at their jobs. Because each of them is holding a slightly different picture of what you're all supposed to be building, and the gap between those pictures is where your week goes.

That's the book. Before I tell you anything, I want to describe the morning, because if I get the morning right you'll trust me with the rest. There are four things pressing on you, and they're not separate problems. They're one problem wearing four faces.

## One: you

Start with the person doing the worrying, because you can't manage anyone else until you've got a handle on yourself, and most of us don't.

You're context switching all day. Corporate-you in the nine o'clock, then father-you on the school-run call, then founder-you in the afternoon pitch, then the version of you that shows up at dinner with nothing left. Each one is a slightly different person, with a slightly different voice, holding a slightly different idea of what matters. None of them is fake. That's the trap. They're all you, and keeping them all running, keeping them all consistent enough that you don't feel like a fraud in at least one of the rooms, costs more than anyone tells you.

The word for what's missing is congruency. When the roles pull in different directions, when work-you and home-you want different things and answer to different values, you spend energy just holding the seams together. And underneath all of it is a quieter problem you don't say out loud: a lot of the busyness is the point. The next deal, the next title, the next fire to put out, it keeps the mind occupied so you don't have to sit still long enough to ask what any of it is for. Busy is a very effective way to avoid the question of meaning. I've used it myself.

So that's the first face. You're exhausted, you're playing too many parts, and you suspect the motion is covering for the fact that you've lost the thread of why.

## Two: the team

Now look up from yourself to the people you're trying to lead, and it splits in two.

The obvious half is alignment: they go off and do the wrong thing, or the right thing in the wrong direction, because nobody's holding the same map. That's real, and we'll get to it. But it's the second half that actually keeps you up, and most leadership books skip straight past it.

The second half is trust. Not the poster on the wall. The real thing: whether you can predict what these people will do when you're not in the room, and whether they can predict the same of you. That doesn't get built in the stand-up. It gets built in the stuff that looks like a waste of time. The reason a good salesperson takes you to dinner and barely mentions the deal is that the deal isn't the point of the dinner. The point is to come away knowing each other well enough to take a risk together later. Most teams have no version of that anymore.

And the ground has shifted underneath it. The team is distributed now, cameras off, scattered across cities and time zones, and the only contact left is the formal channel: the ticket, the status update, the scheduled call with an agenda. All the informal contact that used to build the understanding has been quietly deleted, and we're surprised the trust went with it. An army that hasn't drilled together scatters under pressure. A team that's only ever met through Jira is an army that's never drilled.

## Three: the shared picture nobody can hold still

Here's the one that hides in plain sight, because it's disguised as ordinary work.

Most of what you do all week isn't the work. It's maintaining a shared understanding of the work. The status meeting, the update, the roll-up, the "quick sync to get everyone on the same page," the technical deep-dive that exists only so that six people end up holding roughly the same model in their heads. I'd put it at ninety percent. Ninety percent of the time in meetings is not producing anything. It's just trying to keep everyone's picture of reality lined up long enough to get one thing built.

And it won't hold still. The minute you've got everyone aligned, someone learns something new, the market moves, a person leaves, and the shared picture starts drifting apart again. In a technical field it's worse, because the thing you're trying to share is genuinely hard to explain, so the cost of keeping it congruent goes up. You're not failing at this. The job is genuinely most of the job. We just don't name it, so we treat the thing that eats the week as an interruption instead of the actual work.

## Four: and now this

And then, on top of all of it, AI arrives.

You've been told to use more of it. There's a number attached, a KPI, how many use cases, how many pipelines in production, and everyone's reporting theirs, and nobody can tell you what value any of it actually threw off. So you've got the activity and not the point, which by now should sound familiar.

Underneath the KPI there are three quieter feelings. One, you're being left behind, everyone else seems to have figured something out that you haven't. Two, you don't know where to start, the tools are right there and you still can't see how they fit your actual job. And three, the one nobody says in the meeting: you're a little afraid the real plan is to replace you. So you open the chatbot, ask it something, get a confident answer with no idea who you are or what you're trying to do, and quietly conclude it's a clever toy. It doesn't have your context. It's not a colleague. Right now it's a smart individual contributor with no idea what room it's standing in, and you can't build a team out of that.

## That's the morning

So that's you, before any answers.

Tired from holding too many versions of yourself together. Lying awake over a team you can't get aligned and aren't sure you trust. Spending the actual working hours not working, but maintaining a shared picture that drifts the moment you look away. And now holding a powerful new tool you've been ordered to use and can't yet make useful.

If that's roughly your morning, then we understand each other, and I can tell you the thing I think connects all four. Because they are not four problems. Look closely and they're the same problem at four sizes: the work of holding a shared reality together, inside one head, between people, across a team, and now with a machine in the room. Everything after this is about that one thing.

But first I needed you to recognise the morning. If you did, turn the page.

## How this book resolves it

I'm not going to leave you sitting in the problem. Here's the shape of the answer, and where in the book each piece lands. The whole thing runs off one engine, the same one underneath all four faces: personhood is not a thing sealed in your skull, it's a verb, something you do continuously with other people, distributed across every head that holds a model of you. Once you see that, each of the four problems turns into something you can actually work on.

The order of the book follows the order of the problem: self, then pair, then group, then machine.

**Part 1 starts inside your own head, and it's the answer to face one.** You feel like the sealed author of your own decisions. You're not. The brain acts first and writes the story afterwards: you're an after-the-fact observer narrating a self into existence, the same way a split-brain patient invents a reason for a choice they didn't consciously make. That sounds bleak and it's actually the relief, because if the self is a running narration rather than a fixed thing, the incongruency between corporate-you and home-you and founder-you isn't a character flaw to fix, it's a story you can deliberately make more congruent. One genuine person across all the roles is a more robust foundation than a facade per room, and it costs less to run. This is where the exhaustion gets addressed: most of the tax is from holding too many inconsistent selves together, and you can stop paying so much of it.

**Part 2 moves to the space between two people, and it's the answer to the team's trust half.** This is the peopling model proper: you carry a model of the other person, and a model of them modelling you, and the relationship is the work of keeping those models maintained. That reframes trust from a feeling into something mechanical and buildable: trust is just a well-maintained model of another person, the ability to predict what they'll do when you're not in the room. Which tells you exactly why the dinner that never mentions the deal, the off-site, the random non-work contact, is not a waste of time. It's the maintenance. Stop it and the models drift and the trust goes with it. The fix for the distributed, cameras-off team isn't more formal channels, it's deliberately rebuilding the informal contact that used to do this for free.

**Part 3 scales the same machinery up to the group, and it's the answer to the shared-context problem and the team's alignment half.** A company is just shared context being maintained across many heads, which is why ninety percent of the week goes into meetings that don't produce anything: they're sync, not work. Once you see that maintaining the shared picture *is* the job rather than an interruption to it, you stop resenting it and start doing it on purpose, with vision, ritual, and the deliberate distribution of context. The drift that pulls the picture apart is normal, not failure, and there are ways to keep it congruent that don't eat the whole calendar.

**Part 4 brings in the machine, and it's the answer to face four.** The reason your chatbot feels like a clever toy is that it reads the culture but doesn't *people*: it models the question and not the person asking it, so it gives you the true answer in the wrong room. The resolution is to give the agent the same things we use on each other, a model of who it is, who it's talking to, and who it is relative to that person in this group, so it stops being a generic individual contributor and becomes a participant. That's the real use, and it answers all three of the AI fears at once. Not labour that replaces you, but a context bridge: the thing that accelerates the alignment work humans are bad at and short on time for, translating one person's understanding into another's the way it already translates one language into another. The KPI was never the point. This is.

Four faces, one engine, four parts. The rest of the book is that engine in detail, and then how to build with it. Turn the page.
