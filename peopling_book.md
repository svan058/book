## Overview

Every book about working with people hands you tactics. Run better meetings. Give clearer feedback. Restructure the org. Adopt the methodology. I've read a stack of them and tried most of it, and the tactics aren't wrong so much as they're aimed a layer too high. They treat coordination as a surface you can smooth over with the right process, when the friction is coming from somewhere underneath.

This book is about that underneath. The actual mechanism by which two people, or two hundred, come to hold roughly the same picture of what is going on. The verb for it is peopling, and most of what we file under management, leadership, alignment, and culture is peopling done well or done badly.

The core claim is small enough to fit in one sentence and large enough to reorganise how you read your own week: everything is about synchronising mental models. You don't actually work with your colleagues. You work with the version of them you carry in your head, and they work with the version of you they carry in theirs, and the work goes well exactly to the degree those two models line up. When they are in sync, things move, people feel understood, decisions stick. When they drift apart you get the whole catalogue: miscommunication, rework, meetings that resolve nothing, the slow leak of trust out of a team.

So the book builds from the inside out. It starts in your own head, with how you actually make decisions, which is not the way you think you do. It moves to how you exist inside other people's heads, and they inside yours, and why that recursive mirror is where most of your stress is quietly hiding. Then it turns to the corporate version of the problem, where the bulk of the work turns out to be synchronisation wearing other names. And it brings in AI, not as the thing that replaces the human in the loop, but as a strange new tool for doing the synchronising. A smart ghost you can hand your context to.

I am not going to pretend leadership is about having the answers. Most of the time you won't have them, and the job isn't to fake it. The real job is duller and harder than that: making sure everyone is carrying roughly the same understanding of what matters and why. Get that one thing right and a surprising number of the problems you were treating as separate turn out to be the same problem.

That is the operating system this book is trying to describe. The rest of it is just how the thing runs.

# Chapter 1
## Inside Your Head: Exploring You

You think you are the one making your decisions. You are not, or at least not in the way it feels from the inside. By the time a choice reaches your conscious mind with your name on it, most of the work is already done. The deciding happened underneath, in the dark, and what you experience as choosing is closer to being handed a result along with a tidy press release explaining why you wanted it all along. You did not write that press release. You are its first reader, and you believe every word.

I want to start here, inside one head, your own, because everything else in this book is this same trick scaled up. If you can see how a single mind manufactures a coherent "you" out of a noisy committee of parts, you will recognise the exact same move when two people try to read each other, and again when an organisation of thousands tries to hold one shared picture of what it is doing. Self, then pair, then group. One engine at three sizes. This chapter is the engine at its smallest, and the smallest is where it is easiest to catch in the act.

So let me show you the engine with the cover off. I had the luck, or the strangeness, of spending years looking at mine through a side door most people never bother to open.

## Reality is a controlled hallucination

The first thing to give up is the idea that your brain is a camera. It is not recording the world and passing you the footage. It is a prediction engine. It runs a model of what should be out there and what is about to happen next, and it checks that model against the thin trickle of data coming in through your senses, correcting only where the two disagree. Most of what you are experiencing right now is not arriving from your eyes and ears. It is being generated from the inside, from memory and expectation, with your senses supplying the occasional edit. You are not seeing the room. You are seeing your brain's best guess about the room, lightly fact-checked.

I know this from the inside, not just from the literature, because I spent a long time studying the version of it that runs when the fact-checking is switched off. I have lucid dreamed since I was a child, a dream in which you know you are dreaming, and in my mid-twenties I started keeping a dream diary, partly to hold onto them and partly to run experiments. A dream is your prediction engine with the sensory feed unplugged, generating the whole world out of nothing but your own memory and imagination. And when you push on it, it shows you its parts.

The tests are reliable once you know them. Flying works, because gravity is something your brain imposes rather than something the dream enforces, so a small jump becomes a launch. Reading does not work: text looks right out of the corner of your eye and falls apart the moment you try to actually read it, because the part of the dream that renders the look of words is not the part that makes meaning, and the two cannot keep their story straight. Mirrors misbehave, the reflection lagging or warping, sometimes peeling off into a new character entirely. And people, much like the people in an AI-generated image today, will not hold still. Watch a stranger in a dream too closely and they morph into a different stranger, because nothing underneath is committed to them being one consistent person.

The unsettling part is how little separates that from waking life. The engine is the same engine. The only real difference is that when you are awake, your senses are feeding it a constant stream of shared, external data, so your private hallucination stays roughly pinned to everyone else's. Anil Seth puts it about as well as it can be put: reality is the hallucination we agree on. If we all see the tree, and we all call it green, then the tree is real, not because your access to it is direct, but because it is a hallucination we are all running in sync. Hold onto that phrase, because the whole book is about what happens to the "we agree on" part when the agreement breaks down.

## The committee in the driver's seat

There is one dream experiment I ran that I have never been able to shake, and it taught me more about who is actually in here than anything I have read since.

I was lucid, driving a four-wheel drive, and I decided to test the thing properly. I steered straight into oncoming traffic at full speed, just to see what the engine would do. Do not try the waking version. Three different things happened at once, and the important detail is that they did not agree with each other. One part of me calmly expected the other car to swerve, and it did not, because the dream had not bothered to model another driver reacting to me. Another part, something older and further down, was genuinely terrified, flooding me with adrenaline, heart slamming, certain I was about to die, even though the conscious part of me knew for a fact that none of it was real. And then the cars simply passed through each other, harmless, probably because I had never been in a crash and the engine had no memory to predict the impact from.

Calm expectation, animal terror, and a physics glitch, all firing in the same instant, in the same head, refusing to line up. That was the lesson. There is no single "me" sitting at the controls. There is a committee. A crowd of sub-personal processes, thoughts and reflexes and feelings, each running its own little model, mostly pulling in the same direction in waking life but perfectly capable of contradicting each other when the pressure is on. What you call "you" is not the members of that committee. It is the running summary they produce, the narration that smooths a dozen competing signals into one apparently unified person who seems to be deciding things.

In a dream the committee falls out of sync and you get to watch it. In the office you get a quieter version of the same thing every time part of you wants to take the deal and part of you is uneasy and you cannot say why. That unease is a committee member with a model you have not consciously read. It is usually worth listening to.

## The story is written after the fact

Now here is the part that should bother you, because it is true while you are awake and feels exactly the opposite of true.

In the cleanest experiments on this, the brain signal that initiates a movement fires measurably before the moment you become consciously aware of deciding to move. The decision is underway before "you" arrive to take credit for it. You are, in a real and slightly humbling sense, an after-the-fact observer of processes already running in your own head. And the brain does the only sensible thing a storytelling organ can do in that situation: it writes a narrative. It generates a plausible account of who you are and why you just did that, and it generates it regardless of what actually happened, because a confident story is more useful to a social animal than an honest "I have no idea why I did that."

I caught my own narrator red-handed once, and it has kept me honest ever since. I had been brought in to rescue a project, running several teams, and I was usually wound tight, talking at a hundred miles an hour. One morning I noticed I was unusually slow and calm. I ran three team standups back to back, deliberately relaxed, and afterwards I told myself a clean little story: I had decided that staying calm would lead to better outcomes, so I had stayed calm. A leadership choice. Mature of me. Then I remembered that I had skipped my coffee that morning and taken L-theanine, having read it was good for flow. I was not calm because I had decided to be calm. I was calm because of a supplement. The decision narrative was pure confabulation, written after the fact, and I had believed it instantly and completely, because that is what the narrator does.

## Why your brain never says "I don't know"

The purest demonstration of this comes from people whose two brain hemispheres have been surgically separated, usually to control severe epilepsy. Because the hemispheres no longer talk to each other, you can feed an instruction to one side without the other side knowing. Tell the mute hemisphere to get up and walk, and the person stands and walks. Then ask the speaking hemisphere, which has no idea why the body just moved, why they did it. It does not say "I don't know." It cannot bear to. It says something like "I was going to get a drink." It invents a reason on the spot, and crucially, it believes the reason. It is not lying. It is confabulating, generating a story to cover a gap, with total sincerity.

You do this. I do this. We act, and then we reach back and manufacture the rationale, and the rationale becomes the official record. We are all, quietly, the speaking hemisphere explaining decisions we did not consciously make.

It is worth noticing that we already have a name for a machine that does exactly this, and we treat it as a flaw. When a language model produces a fluent, confident, completely fabricated answer, we call it a hallucination and file it under "things to fix." But confabulation is not a bug the machines have and we do not. It is the native mode of the human narrator. We have just had a lifetime of practice and a constant feed of correcting data, so our fabrications usually land closer to the truth. The mechanism is the same. I will come back to that symmetry later in the book, because it turns out to matter enormously for what these systems are and are not, but for now just sit with the discomfort that the thing we mock in the machine is the thing running your sense of self.

## You are the story, and you are not the only author

So if a large part of who you are is a running self-narration, stitched together after the fact from the output of a committee you cannot fully see, then "the real you" is not some fixed nugget buried in there waiting to be found. You are the story your brain keeps telling about itself. That sounds like bad news and it is actually the opening this whole book walks through. A story can be edited. A fixed essence cannot. The fact that the self is narrated rather than carved in stone is precisely why you can become more coherent on purpose, which is what the back half of this book is for.

But there is one more turn, and it is the one that breaks you out of your own skull and sets up everything that follows. You are the main character of your narration. You are not the only character in it. Your story is populated. It runs on a cast of other people, the version of your boss you carry around, the version of your partner, the version of the colleague you are quietly worried about, each of them a little model you maintain and consult and argue with even when they are nowhere near you. You are not only narrating yourself. You are simulating everyone you deal with, and they are doing the same to you.

That is where the engine gets interesting, and where most of your stress at work is actually hiding. We have looked inside one head. The next chapter steps into the space between two, where you discover that a person was never sealed inside a single skull to begin with.
