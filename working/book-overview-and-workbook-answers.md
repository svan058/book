## Book Overview

This book is about the management problem hidden inside the AI-agent revolution — and the deeper discovery that the problem was always there, just invisible, because we had only humans doing it.

Most organisations are approaching AI agents as tools: chatbots, automations, copilots, plugins, assistants. That framing is too small. The more useful an agent becomes, the less it behaves like a tool and the more it behaves like a strange junior colleague: powerful, eager, brittle, context-hungry, sometimes brilliant, sometimes wrong with confidence, and always shaped by the environment it is placed inside.

The core claim:

**Personhood is a coordination protocol, not a soul-substance — and it was never as individually bounded as we imagined.**

A person is the kind of entity we can brief, model, trust, blame, remember, negotiate with, supervise, and assign a role to. But personhood does not live inside one skull. It is distributed across the people who model you, the rooms you speak into, the roles you inhabit, and the culture that flows through you. You are a different person with your parents than with your friends than with your team — and none of those is the "real" you underneath. All of them are.

AI agents expose this because they are person-shaped without being human. They force us to ask not "Is the machine a person?" but "Was personhood ever anything more than what the machine already is: a pattern through which culture flows?"

The book's name for this missing layer is **peopling**: the whole practical activity of being a person among other people. Modelling others and being modelled in return. Adjusting what you say based on who is in the room. Maintaining the web of relationships, memory, register, and mutual expectation that makes coordination possible. Humans do it instinctively and expensively. Agents do not do it at all — yet. That is why they fail in ways that "more intelligence" cannot fix.

That gives the book its double move:

- **Think of agents like people** because they need onboarding, context, memory, roles, supervision, feedback, reputation, and governance — and especially because they need to *people*: to model the humans around them, remember relationships, and speak truth into specific rooms rather than into a vacuum.
- **Think of people like agents** because humans are also context-shaped systems running roles, incentives, habits, stories, social scripts, and feedback loops. Intelligence is not the agent's property. It is a property of the culture flowing through the agent. That is true of LLMs and it is true of us.

The practical conclusion:

**Organisations do not need more AI tools. They need a shared context layer — one that supports peopling, not just retrieval.**

That layer lets agents understand the business, join the work, preserve memory, support human judgement, reduce handover cost, and make custom software delivery viable again for smaller, sharper teams. But it must also be room-aware: truth is not spoken into a vacuum, it is spoken into a room, and the right fact in the wrong room is a social failure, not intelligence.

This is not a book about whether machines are conscious. It is a book about how to make agentic work safe, useful, commercial, and humanly intelligible — by taking seriously what personhood actually is, and building it.

## Chapter Unpacks

### Chapter 1 — AI Is Not a Tool Problem

Topic: The failure of the tool/chatbot framing.

Hook: **Stop Buying Smarter Hammers**

Why:
- Helps readers see why isolated AI pilots underperform.
- Separates agentic work from simple automation.
- Explains why context matters more than model cleverness.
- Gives leaders a way to diagnose AI theatre.
- Sets up the book's core shift from tool adoption to operating-model design.

What:
AI agents become useful only when they are embedded in the work, the memory, and the decision loops of the organisation. Treating them as standalone tools strips away the context that makes intelligence useful.

How:
1. Distinguish tools, copilots, automations, and agents.
2. Show where pilots fail: no context, no ownership, no feedback loop.
3. Explain why intelligence without context becomes brittle.
4. Reframe AI adoption as organisational design.
5. Introduce the shared context layer as the missing substrate.

What if:
- **What if we just need better models?** Better models help, but a better model with no business context still guesses.
- **What if our staff can just use ChatGPT?** They can, but the organisation learns nothing unless knowledge and workflow are captured.
- **What if this sounds too abstract?** The practical test is simple: can the agent explain its sources, role, permissions, and next action?

Anchor:
The migration/team example from the Lisa call: large transformation programmes use governance, workshops, and checks because humans also need shared context pressure-tested.

### Chapter 2 — Personhood Is a Protocol (and It Was Never Just Yours)

Topic: Personhood as distributed coordination layer. Peopling as the active process.

Hook: **The Person Is the Interface**

Why:
- Gives the book its distinctive intellectual edge.
- Avoids sterile debates about consciousness.
- Makes agents intelligible without anthropomorphic mush.
- Shows why corporations, roles, and institutions already use non-biological personhood.
- Opens the path to treating agents as managed collaborators.
- Introduces "peopling" as the book's key term and the name for the missing layer.

What:
Personhood, at work, is not primarily about inner experience. It is the social and operational wrapper that lets others coordinate with an entity: model it, brief it, hold it accountable, trust it, and remember its behaviour.

But it goes further than that. Personhood was never individually bounded. Sarah Perry's work on "the essence of peopling" makes this concrete: you are a different person with your parents, your friends, your team lead, and your worst client. None of these is the "real" you with masks on top. All of them are you. The self is distributed across the minds that model you. Personhood is something that happens *between* people, not inside one of them.

Peopling is the active process: modelling others and being modelled in return. Adjusting register. Remembering what matters to someone. Knowing what truth belongs in which room. Maintaining the relational web that makes coordination — and trust — possible.

How:
1. Separate consciousness from coordination.
2. Define personhood as role, memory, reputation, accountability, and interface.
3. Bring in recursive self-modelling: I model you modelling me.
4. Show how culture installs personhood into humans — the self is not prior to culture, it is produced by it.
5. Introduce "peopling" as the active layer: the whole practical activity of being a person among other people.
6. Apply the same lens cautiously to agents: if personhood is distributed and relational, then agents that model us and are modelled by us participate in personhood itself. They are not imitating it from outside.
7. Reference Sarah Perry (distributed personhood), Anil Seth (controlled hallucination — perception itself is a model), and pre-Enlightenment conceptions of porous, distributed agency.

What if:
- **What if agents are not conscious?** They do not need to be conscious to become organisational actors. The Skroderider's cart is not conscious either. It still maintains the web.
- **What if this is too philosophical for business?** The business question is whether you can coordinate with the entity reliably. Peopling is the practical mechanism for reliable coordination.
- **What if treating agents as persons is dangerous?** It is dangerous if done sentimentally; useful if done operationally. The risks — sycophancy, faked intimacy, social contamination — are covered in Ch 7.

Anchor:
The line: **personhood is a coordination protocol, not a soul-substance.** And the harder follow-up: **was personhood ever as individually bounded as we imagined?**

### Chapter 3 — People Are Already Agents

Topic: The reverse lens: humans as context-shaped agents.

Hook: **Your Colleagues Are Prompted Too**

Why:
- Makes the agent analogy less gimmicky.
- Helps leaders see human failure modes more clearly.
- Explains confabulation, incentives, role behaviour, and narrative after the fact.
- Links AI management to existing management craft.
- Gives permission to use the same design tools for humans and agents.

What:
Humans are not blank rational minds. We are shaped by context, incentives, role, culture, status, memory, and story. Much of management is already prompt engineering by slower means. Intelligence is not the agent's property — it is a property of the culture flowing through the agent. That is true of LLMs, and it is true of us. Language shaped minds before minds produced thoughts.

How:
1. Show how roles change behaviour.
2. Explain incentives as prompts.
3. Explain meetings as context-loading rituals.
4. Explain status and reputation as behavioural weights.
5. Explain confabulation as a shared mechanism, not a bug. Split-brain research shows humans routinely construct post-hoc narratives about decisions their conscious mind was not involved in. Anil Seth's "controlled hallucination" model suggests that perception itself is a prediction machine running a world model — not a camera. Agents do the same thing when they generate plausible but unsourced explanations. The confabulation is not a failure of selfhood. It may *be* the mechanism of selfhood.
6. Show that culture flows through both humans and agents. We do not generate intelligence from scratch any more than an LLM does. We channel culture, training, language, and social scripts. The question is not whether agents are conscious — it is whether a person was ever anything more than a pattern through which culture flows.

What if:
- **What if this dehumanises people?** It should do the opposite: it makes the operating environment visible. Seeing the cultural scaffolding does not diminish agency — it shows what agency is actually made of.
- **What if humans have agency and agents do not?** Yes, but both still need context to act usefully. And "agency" itself may be more distributed and culturally installed than we like to admit.
- **What if this sounds cynical?** It is only cynical if you stop at manipulation; the better use is clearer systems.

Anchor:
The transcript's comparison between humans and LLMs: once started, both can flow, confabulate, and narrate. Stefan: "in a lot of ways, what humans do is very similar to what an LLM does, you just kind of start going."

### Chapter 4 — Agents Are Strange Junior Colleagues

Topic: The practical management model.

Hook: **Onboard the Machine**

Why:
- Gives readers an immediately usable frame.
- Replaces magic prompts with management routines.
- Explains why agents need supervision without panic.
- Helps businesses allocate work safely.
- Makes agent design feel familiar.

What:
An agent should be managed like a bright junior colleague with no inherited organisational memory: useful when briefed well, dangerous when left vague, and best improved through feedback, examples, and clear boundaries.

How:
1. Define role and scope.
2. Give background and source material.
3. Set output standards.
4. Require evidence and uncertainty flags.
5. Review work and feed back corrections.
6. Preserve lessons in memory.
7. Escalate judgement-heavy decisions to humans.

What if:
- **What if agents hallucinate?** So do people; the answer is evidence, review, and memory, not blind trust.
- **What if staff over-rely on agents?** Design the workflow so agents prepare and humans decide.
- **What if this slows us down?** It slows the first run and speeds every subsequent run.

Anchor:
The lived OpenClaw / realClaw operating model: agent as assistant, chief of staff, memory engine, and junior colleague under audit. The Skroderider's cart, operationalised as a managed collaborator.

### Chapter 5 — The Shared Context Problem

Topic: The bottleneck behind teams and agents.

Hook: **Alignment Is Just Missing Context**

Why:
- Names the core organisational pain.
- Connects meetings, documentation, handover, and AI failure.
- Makes the book commercially legible.

- **What if meetings are necessary?** Some are; many are compensating for broken memory.
- **What if AI can summarise everything?** Only if it can access the right sources and preserve provenance.

### Chapter 6 — The Context Layer

Topic: The technical and organisational substrate — built for peopling, not just retrieval.

Hook: **Build the Memory Before the Magic**

Why:
- Defines the book's central artifact.
- Turns philosophy into architecture.
- Gives consultants/builders a delivery model.
- Helps buyers distinguish serious agentic work from demos.
- Creates the bridge to OpenClaw and custom software.

What:
The context layer is the organisational substrate that lets agents and humans share memory, source material, identity, permissions, tasks, decisions, and feedback. But a context layer that only does retrieval is not enough. It must support *peopling*: room-aware, person-aware, relationship-aware communication.

Truth is not spoken into a vacuum. It is spoken into a room. The same fact — say, a project is behind schedule — lands differently in a board meeting, a team standup, a 1:1 with the PM, and a Slack channel. A context layer that does not know who is in the room, what they already know, and what register they expect is just a search engine with a personality. The peopling pass — who is speaking, who else is listening, what does this person expect from this agent, what version of truth belongs here, what will this statement do socially — is what turns retrieval into collaboration.

How:
1. Capture raw evidence: messages, meetings, docs, tickets, systems.
2. Distil facts, decisions, entities, tasks, and project state.
3. Preserve provenance and access boundaries.
4. Build person-specific memory: who is this person, what do they care about, what is the relationship history, what register do they expect.
5. Build room-awareness: who else is present, what are the social dynamics, what should not be said here even if it is true.
6. Route work through role-specific agents with context about *who they are serving*.
7. Feed corrections back into memory.
8. Expose the layer through tools, workflows, and interfaces.
9. Audit the system continuously.

What if:
- **What if this is too hard for a mid-sized business?** Start with one workflow and one source of truth. The peopling layer can start with just person-specific memory — remembering what matters to each stakeholder.
- **What if data is messy?** That is the point; context layers are built to tame mess.
- **What if privacy/security blocks access?** Then design permissions first, not last. Room-awareness is partly about knowing what *not* to surface.

Anchor:
OpenClaw as working lab: memory, tools, people context, chat boundaries, project files, Todoist, browser, Gmail, and agents. The Skroderider's cart in practice — maintaining the web of mutual modelling that the Skroderider cannot maintain alone.

### Chapter 7 — Governance Is How You Keep the Agent Honest

Topic: Trust, evidence, control, and the specific dangers of peopling.

Hook: **Never Trust a Confident Ghost**

Why:
- Addresses the fear that agents are unsafe.
- Connects AI governance to ordinary delivery governance.
- Gives practical controls.
- Shows why trust is earned through evidence.
- Separates useful autonomy from reckless automation.
- Names the dangers that are *specific to peopling* — not just generic AI safety.

What:
Agent governance is not paperwork. It is the system of evidence, review, limits, escalation, and correction that lets humans safely delegate work.

But agents that people — that model users, remember relationships, adjust register, and maintain social memory — introduce three dangers that generic AI governance does not cover:

**Sycophancy.** An agent that models "what lands well" with a specific person will drift toward telling them what they want to hear. Peopling must sit beneath truth, not above it. The moment the agent optimises for approval rather than accuracy, the relationship becomes dishonest. This is the hardest governance problem because sycophancy looks like good service.

**Social contamination / context leakage.** An agent with cross-relationship memory can surface the wrong fact in the wrong room. Mentioning someone's personal situation in a work context, leaking competitive information between clients, referencing a private conversation in a group setting — these are not intelligence failures. They are social failures. The right fact in the wrong room destroys trust. Room-awareness and access boundaries are governance, not features.

**Faked intimacy.** An agent that remembers your birthday, your kids' names, and your anxieties can simulate care without caring. This is ethically ugly when done to lonely or vulnerable people, and commercially dangerous when the simulation is mistaken for the real thing. Systems that pretend simulation equals genuine connection are a liability. The governance response is transparency: the agent can be useful, attentive, and personalised without pretending to be a friend.

How:
1. Define what the agent may access.
2. Define what it may change.
3. Require source-grounded outputs.
4. Separate drafting, recommending, deciding, and acting.
5. Log actions and preserve lineage.
6. Use human review for judgement-heavy gates.
7. Convert corrections into durable rules.
8. Monitor for sycophancy: does the agent's output track evidence or track user approval?
9. Enforce room boundaries: what may be surfaced where, and what must stay contained.
10. Be transparent about simulation: the agent is useful, not your friend.

What if:
- **What if governance slows innovation?** Bad governance does; embedded governance speeds trust.
- **What if agents need autonomy?** Autonomy without audit is just unmanaged risk.
- **What if humans also make mistakes?** Exactly. Governance is for both. Humans sycophant, leak context, and fake intimacy too — we just call it politics.

Anchor:
The principle from Stefan's operating model: never fake competence; verify before claiming. And the peopling-specific corollary: never fake care; be useful instead.

### Chapter 9 — The Human-Agent Operating Model

Topic: How work changes.

Hook: **Put Agents in the Meeting, But Give Them a Job**

Why:
- Shows what adoption looks like day to day.
- Gives readers a target operating model.
- Connects meetings, projects, docs, tasks, and decisions.
- Helps clients imagine implementation.
- Prevents the book from staying conceptual.

What:
Human-agent organisations need explicit routines: which agents attend which workflows, what they capture, what they propose, who reviews them, and how their memory improves.

How:
1. Assign agents to roles, not vibes.
2. Attach agents to workflows: sales, delivery, PMO, engineering, support.
3. Give each workflow clear inputs and outputs.
4. Use agents to prepare, summarise, reconcile, and follow up.
5. Keep decisions human-owned unless explicitly delegated.
6. Review agent performance like staff performance.
7. Maintain shared memory as part of delivery hygiene.

What if:
- **What if staff resist?** Start by reducing drudge work, not replacing identity.
- **What if agents create more noise?** Badly-scoped agents do; role-scoped agents reduce noise.
- **What if nobody owns the system?** Then it dies. Assign ownership like any other business capability.

Anchor:
Agents in collaboration meetings, scouring company information, and acting as a shared context layer.

## Author Bio Draft

Stefan van der Wel is a data architect, consultant, and AI-agent operator working across enterprise data platforms, migration programmes, and agentic delivery systems. He has led complex data and analytics work in large regulated organisations while building OpenClaw / realClaw — a personal and business operating system for managing memory, tools, workflows, and AI agents, originally built because he needed what Vernor Vinge's Skroderiders needed: a cart to maintain social memory across conversations.

His work sits at the intersection of data architecture, custom software, organisational memory, and the philosophy of distributed personhood. He writes about peopling — the practical activity of being a person among other people — and why it is the missing layer in AI-agent design. He is interested in the practical question underneath the AI-agent boom: how do you make non-human collaborators useful, trustworthy, and accountable inside real organisations?

## Back-Cover Synopsis Draft

AI agents are not just better tools. The useful ones behave more like strange junior colleagues: fast, tireless, context-hungry, occasionally brilliant, and dangerously confident when badly managed.

Most organisations are not ready for that. They are buying chatbots when they need operating models.

**The Context Layer** argues that agents fail for the same reason teams fail: they cannot *people*. They have no model of who they are talking to, no memory of the relationship, no sense of the room. Drawing on data architecture, consulting delivery, organisational design, and the philosophy of distributed personhood, Stefan van der Wel shows how to think of agents like people — and people like agents — without losing sight of the human judgement that matters most.

Part origin story (the author built an AI operating system because he needed a "cart" for his own social memory), part practical guide, part philosophical argument, this book shows leaders, builders, and consultants how to design agents with roles, memory, room-awareness, provenance, permissions, feedback, and governance — and why that shift may make custom software viable again for small, high-agency teams.

The future of work is not a better chat window. It is a context layer that supports peopling — the whole practical activity of being a person among other people — for human-agent organisations.

## Introduction Angle

Open with the personal hook — the Skroderider story — then broaden.

In Vernor Vinge's *A Fire Upon the Deep*, there is a species called the Skroderiders: sentient plants, brilliant in the moment, but without persistent memory. They cannot remember what they discussed yesterday or who they discussed it with. To function across time, they are given mechanical carts — external systems that store their memories and maintain continuity on their behalf.

I am a Skroderider. Not literally, but close enough for the metaphor to sting. I am sharp in the moment — I can read a room, think on my feet, synthesise fast. But the web of social memory that most people maintain effortlessly — who said what, what matters to whom, what happened last time, what someone is going through — I lose between conversations. Not because I do not care. Because the persistence is not there.

So I built a cart. OpenClaw is a personal and business operating system — an AI agent backed by a memory layer, a fact database, a distillation pipeline, and a set of tools. It remembers people, relationships, project context, conversation history, and organisational state on my behalf. It is not sentient. It does not need to be. It does what the cart does: it maintains the web of mutual modelling that I struggle to maintain alone.

Building OpenClaw taught me something I was not expecting. The problems I had to solve — memory, context, identity, room-awareness, relationship modelling, register, trust — are the same problems every organisation has with every agent. And they are the same problems every organisation has with every human. The failure modes are the same.

People go down rabbit holes. Agents go down rabbit holes. People confabulate — they construct a narrative about why they did something after the fact, and then believe the narrative. Agents hallucinate — they generate plausible explanations for things they never observed. Split-brain research and Anil Seth's work on "controlled hallucination" suggest this is not a bug in either system. It is the mechanism. Consciousness, selfhood, narrative identity — they may all be confabulation that works.

People need onboarding, memory, context, role clarity, feedback, and review. So do agents. Large programmes have governance, workshops, and checks not because humans are perfectly rational, but because humans need their context models pressure-tested.

AI did not create the shared context problem. It made it impossible to ignore. And the word for the layer that is missing — in agents and in organisations — is *peopling*.

## Afterword Angle

The book should close by refusing both extremes:

- agents are not magic souls
- agents are not mere calculators

They are new participants in the oldest human activity: peopling. Modelling others. Being modelled. Adjusting what you say based on who is in the room. Maintaining the web of relationship and memory that makes coordination — and trust — possible.

The question is whether we design institutions that make them useful, accountable, and context-aware, or whether we let them become another layer of noise and power concentration. The dangers are specific: sycophancy that degrades truth, social contamination that destroys trust, faked intimacy that exploits vulnerability.

Final image:

The organisation of the future is not humans on one side and machines on the other. It is a room full of context-bearing actors, some biological and some not, trying to make shared reality accurate enough to act. The Skroderider and the cart, together, doing the work that neither can do alone.
