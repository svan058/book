# Draft: The Google Maps analogy (context bridge)

Watch what people in a company actually do all day and you find that most of it is not the work. It is getting onto the same page about the work. The vision, the alignment, the values, the stand-ups, the re-syncs, the "quick catch-up just to make sure we're aligned": strip the corporate paint off all of it and the same thing is underneath. Everyone is trying to hold the same picture in their head at the same time, and the picture keeps drifting, so they keep meeting to drag it back. We call this collaboration. Mostly it is maintenance. Mostly it is sync.

The analogy that finally made this land for a friend of mine is Google Maps. Maps knows what is going on out on the roads, and notice that it does not know because anyone wrote it down. It knows because everyone driving is quietly feeding their position into one live picture, and that picture, assembled out of everybody's ordinary traffic, is something no single driver could ever hold. You cannot see the jam four streets ahead. Maps can, because Maps is standing above the whole map. So in other words: the intelligence is not in any one car. It is in the aggregate, and the car just gets the benefit of being routed around a snarl it had no way of seeing coming.

A context-bearing agent is that, pointed at an organisation instead of a city. It is built the same way, as a group effort out of everyone's ordinary traffic, every message and document and decision that was going to be produced anyway. It is aware the same way, across the whole map rather than from inside one role. And it does the same job: it routes a person around the jam they cannot see. The finance director does not need to read the three threads where the data model changed. The agent already read them, and it can hand her the one turn she needs to take. Nobody has to spend the morning synchronising by hand, because the synchronising has become infrastructure, the way knowing which roads are busy stopped being a thing you asked the bloke at the petrol station and became a thing your phone simply holds.

This matters more now than it would have ten years ago, and I think the reason is mundane. We used to absorb a lot of this picture for free, in the corridor, at the desk next door, in the managed daycare of an open-plan office where ideas leaked between people whether or not a meeting was booked. Since Covid most of that informal traffic is gone. The third place where context used to spread on its own has thinned out, people are more isolated, and the shared picture decays faster than anyone refills it. So the thing that was ambient now has to be built on purpose. That is the gap the bridge is filling: not a clever new capability, but the connective tissue an office used to provide and quietly stopped providing.

I want to be careful not to oversell the cleverness here. The agent is not smarter than the people in the rooms. It is a smart cultural ghost, repeating back the organisation's own knowledge in the organisation's own words, and the only real trick is that it was in every room and no human was. That is the whole edge: not intelligence, position. Google Maps is not a better driver than you. It has just seen the whole map, and you are looking at one street.
