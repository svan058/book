This is also why a mind on its own does not behave like one. You are already a populated place. The committee from a few pages ago is mostly other people you have taken inside: the parent whose verdict you still flinch at, the mentor you argue with in the shower, the friend you ask, silently, what they would make of a decision. That is peopling run inward, and it is doing real work even when you are alone in a room. The striking thing is what happens when you take the real people away entirely. The machinery does not switch off. It manufactures them. Joshua Slocum, the first man to sail alone around the world, fell ill in a gale off the Azores and woke to find a stranger at the wheel: the pilot of the Pinta, who told him to lie still and steered the Spray through the night while he sweated out the fever. Slocum knew it was not real and was grateful anyway. The same figure turns up wherever people are pushed far enough alone, the unseen companion at the edge of survival, the extra walker Shackleton's exhausted party kept counting across South Georgia and could never find.

You can produce a milder version in a laboratory in about a minute. Sit someone at a device that touches them on the back exactly as they move their own hand, then slip in a half-second delay, and they will feel, eyes open and nothing behind them, that another person is in the room. The presence is not a stranger. It is them, copied and set down a few feet away, because the machinery built to model other people has no one to model, so it turns on the only person available. That is how load-bearing peopling is. It is not a social skill laid on top of a solitary self. It is what a self is made of, and a mind starved of others will sooner conjure one than run alone.

It does not always conjure a kindly sailor. In 1968 Donald Crowhurst entered a solo round-the-world race in a boat that could not make it, and rather than face the ruin of quitting he stopped in the Atlantic and began faking the voyage in a second logbook, meaning to slip back into the fleet as a quiet last-place finisher nobody would examine. The lie closed around him. The real leader, racing a Crowhurst who was not there, drove his own boat to pieces and dropped out, which left Crowhurst certain to win, and winning meant his logs would be read by experts and the fraud exposed. Trapped, and alone for months, he opened a logbook he headed Philosophy and filled twenty-five thousand words with gods: life was a "game" run by cosmic beings, he decided he could become one of them, shed his body and leave the game for good. With no real people left to keep his invented ones honest, those were the gods he followed off the back of the boat. The same reflex that hands a drowning sailor a companion will, left unchecked, hand a cornered man a religion to die into.
