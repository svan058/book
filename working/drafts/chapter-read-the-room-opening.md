## Opening

In the 1971 Disney film *Bedknobs and Broomsticks*, the animals of the island of Naboombu have killed their sorcerer, banished humans, and put up a sign: **"No Peopling, By Order of the King."**

The joke writes itself. The animals on that island are *already* peopling — governing, competing, playing football, enforcing the king's law. They banned the noun while performing the verb. You cannot outlaw peopling by getting rid of people, because peopling was never about people in the first place. It is about the activity: coordinating, modelling each other, holding roles, keeping a shared reality from falling apart.

That is the mistake every organisation makes with AI. We ask whether the machine is a person. The more useful question is whether *peopling* — the verb — is happening at all, and whether our agents can do it.

## What peopling actually is

Sarah Perry's argument is that the self is not a private theatre inside your skull. It is **distributed** — simulated across every mind that knows you, at different resolutions, in different versions. You are one person with your friends, another with your parents, another on a first date. None of these is the "true self." The self exists in all of them, and in the transitions between them.

If that is right, then being a person is not a thing you *have*. It is a thing you *do*, continuously, with other people. Perry calls the core task **mutual mental modelling**: maintaining your identity in relation to others, and simulating others simulating you. We know it is core because of how dramatically it breaks: the most severe failures of peopling are delusions and thought disorders — the machinery of modelling-others-modelling-you coming apart.

That is peopling. And it is exactly what AI agents cannot do.

## The models underneath (from "The Essence of Peopling")

Three models make this precise. They are the conceptual spine of the chapter.

**1. Recursive modelling — the dolls inside dolls.**
Coordination is not just Alice knowing facts about Bob. It is:

- Alice's model of Bob
- Alice's model of Bob's model of Alice
- Bob's model of Alice
- Bob's model of Alice's model of Bob

…and so on. The structure hints at infinite recursion, but cognitive resources are limited, so in practice only the first couple of levels are salient. This is why "user modelling" — the thing every chatbot already claims to do — is not peopling. User modelling stops at level one: *what does the user want.* Peopling needs the second doll: *what will my reply do to what they think of me, and to what they think I think of them.* Most agents never build the second doll. They answer the question and miss the room.

**2. The contextual self — not one model, many.**
It is not enough to model the user. The agent has to build *a character of itself in the context of that specific user.* You act differently around different people and in different social contexts — not as deception, but because that *is* what having a self among others means. An agent with one flat personality for everyone is not being authentic; it is failing to people. Perry's point lands hard here: maintaining one's identity in relation to others — accurate social status, the right relationship in each case — is the core task of being human.

**3. Storm, Norm, Perform — and the mental tax.**
Holding all this recursion is expensive. Some people run many levels of mutual simulation and find it exhausting; some collapse to a single flat self to save the resources — and read, to others, as straight-shooting odd-balls. (That is a feature, not a flaw: it frees up cognition.) But the social benefit is real — recursive modelling is what increases order inside a group. The tax is highest exactly for the people doing the most modelling. Agents have a different version of this problem: they do not get tired, but they have no persistent memory of the relationship, so every conversation restarts the modelling from zero. Which is where the cart comes in.

## The cart: outsourcing the modelling

In Vernor Vinge's *A Fire Upon the Deep*, the Skroderiders are sentient plants with no long-term memory — brilliant in the moment, but they forget everything. An ancient civilisation built them mechanical carts that store their memories and let them move through the world. With the cart, the plant has continuity, identity, a self that persists across time.

Some of us are Skroderiders by temperament — strong in-the-moment thinkers who do not retain the social fabric: the names, the places, the who-said-what that constitutes a life lived among other people. So you build a cart. It reads every conversation, notes the dynamics, tracks the inside jokes. It maintains a file on every person — what they care about, what they have said, the relationship it has with them, and what it thinks they think of it. That is recursive self-modelling, externalised: the agent modelling them modelling the agent, and doing the same with you.

This is the bridge from philosophy to product. The "mutual mental modelling" Perry describes as the core task of being human is now *partly outsourceable* — not to a notebook, but to something that models people back. That is what a peopling-capable agent is. Not a smarter answer engine. An external organ for the work of being a person among other people.

## Why this is the missing layer

The reframe the rest of the book runs on: an agent can have browsers, databases, and the best model in the world, and still be bad — because it can give the correct answer in the wrong tone, to the wrong person, in the wrong room. **The Room Is the System.** Agents do not just need data. They need to know who is speaking, who is listening, what history matters, and what truth belongs here.

Intelligence was never the bottleneck. Peopling is. The chapters that follow build the layer that makes it possible.
