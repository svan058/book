# The Context Layer

## Introduction: We Think, Therefore I Am

Somewhere around three in the morning, a lot of capable people are lying awake running the same loop. Not over the work itself. Over the people: how to get Johnny and Jimmy and the rest of them pointing the same direction by Thursday, how to hold a team together that keeps drifting apart, how to be the person their job needs them to be when they barely recognise that person anymore. The work was never the hard part. The people were always the hard part.

This is a book about why, and the answer turns out to be older and stranger than any management theory. It starts with a question I've been circling most of my life, which is what a self actually is, and it ends somewhere useful, which is how you build a team that doesn't fall apart. The bridge between the two is a single idea: that being a person is not a thing you are, it's a thing you do, together, all the time, and most of the exhaustion in modern life comes from doing it badly or alone.

Let me start where I started, which is with the self.

We carry around a picture of ourselves as a sealed unit. There's an "I" in here, behind the eyes, the real you, and everyone else is outside, and you spend your life expressing this inner self to the world and occasionally being misunderstood by it. Descartes gave us the cleanest version: *I think, therefore I am.* The one thing you can't doubt is the lone mind, alone in its skull, proving its own existence to itself.

I think that's backwards, and so does a growing body of people who've looked closely. The philosopher Sarah Perry, in an essay on what she calls the essence of peopling, treats *people* as a verb rather than a noun, and once you sit with that the whole picture flips. You don't start as a private self that then learns to deal with others. You get built into a self by other people being aware of you, the way you only ever learn your own face from a mirror. The infant becomes a person by being treated as one. Self-consciousness is something other people install in you. The older, truer version of Descartes runs the other way round: *we think, therefore I am.*

This isn't only a modern academic point; it's a historical one. The intense, sovereign individual we take for granted is a fairly recent invention, something that hardened up around the Protestant Reformation. Go back further and people understood themselves as part of a group first, and understood their own inner life as far more porous. In the old stories, in Homer and the Hebrew Bible, emotions arrive from outside as forces: courage is put into you by a god, a dark spirit comes upon a king. That sounds primitive until you notice it might be the more honest description. We don't really author our moods either. They come and go, and we narrate ourselves as their owner after the fact. The ancients just kept the books more truthfully.

So who you are is not one fixed thing in one skull. It's distributed. There's the you in your own head, and a version of you in your friend's head, another in your mother's, another in the head of someone you met last week. Each runs at a different resolution and behaves a little differently, because each is tuned to the particular relationship. You're one person with your mates, another with your parents, another on a first date. None of those is the real you wearing costumes. The self lives across all of them at once, and it lives in the gaps between them.

And maintaining it is *work*. Perry's word for the core of it is recursive self-modelling: you hold a model of who you are in relation to others, while modelling them modelling you. My picture of you. My picture of your picture of me. And on down. In theory it runs forever; in practice we've got limited cognition, so only the first couple of levels are ever really live. But that second level is the whole game of being socially competent. Most people who struggle with others aren't unkind, they just aren't running the second level: they answer the question and miss the room, because they never modelled the room. And holding all that modelling costs something real. The people doing the most of it are often the most tired, which is why some retreat to a handful of close relationships and let the rest go. They're not antisocial. They've just noticed the bill.

That bill is the first thread of this book, and it runs straight into the life most professionals are actually living.

Because look at what we ask a person to do now. You context-switch all day between selves that don't quite agree: the corporate version in the nine o'clock, the parent on the school-run call, the founder in the afternoon pitch, the person who's supposed to have something left at dinner. Each is a slightly different character with slightly different values, and keeping them all running, keeping them consistent enough that you don't feel like a fraud in at least one room, is a tax most people pay without ever naming it. The word for what goes missing is congruency. When the roles pull against each other, you spend your energy holding the seams together instead of on anything that matters. And underneath it sits a quieter thing nobody says out loud: a lot of the busyness is the point. The next deal, the next title, the next fire to fight, it keeps the mind occupied so you never have to sit still long enough to ask what any of it is for. Busy is a very effective anaesthetic. I've used it myself.

The second thread is what happens when you scale a self up to a relationship, and then to a team.

If a person is a distributed model that has to be maintained, then so is a friendship, a marriage, a working relationship. Stop putting contact in and the models drift, and you get the thing people describe as "we just couldn't really connect," which almost always means they never finished building a model of each other. This is why trust is far less mystical than we make it. The off-sites, the team dinners, the rapport-building that looks like a waste of a good afternoon, what we're actually doing when we "build trust" is seeing each other from enough angles to predict what the other person will do when we're not in the room. Trust is a well-maintained model of another person. The reason a good salesperson takes you out and barely mentions the deal is that the dinner *is* the work. It's model maintenance.

And here's where it bites in the modern workplace. We evolved for this in tribes of roughly a hundred and fifty, where everyone knew everyone instinctively because they'd lived alongside each other for years. We don't have that. We barely know the people we work with, so we've fallen back on formal, procedural substitutes for what used to be instinct: the ticket, the status update, the scheduled call with an agenda. The team is distributed now, cameras off, scattered across cities, and the only contact left is the formal channel. All the informal stuff that used to do the maintenance for free, the corner by the coffee machine, the walk to the station, the church and the club and the third places that have quietly thinned out, especially since Covid, has been deleted, and we're surprised the trust went with it. An army that's never drilled together scatters under the first pressure. A team that's only ever met through a project-management tool is an army that's never drilled.

The third thread is the one hiding in plain sight, because it's disguised as ordinary work. Most of what you do in an organisation isn't the work. It's maintaining a shared picture of the work. Sit in a week of meetings and notice what they're for: status updates, milestones, walk-throughs, rolling things up, aligning stakeholders. Almost none of it produces anything directly. It's the distribution of shared context so that a group of separate minds can act as one thing. I'd put it at something like ninety percent of corporate life being the synchronising of a shared model toward an outcome. I'm making the exact number up, but the shape is right, and once you see it you can't unsee it. The hard part of any ambitious project is not the doing. It's getting the same picture into a dozen heads and keeping it there while the world keeps knocking it out of alignment. An army that isn't drilled on the plan scatters; a football team where nobody knows their position loses, however good the individual players. Coordination is the scarce thing, not talent.

So that's the situation, before anyone offers a solution. You're tired from holding too many versions of yourself together. You're losing sleep over a team you can't quite align and aren't sure you trust. And the actual working hours go not into the work but into maintaining a shared understanding that drifts the moment you look away. Three problems, and by the time we're done I want to show you they're not three at all. They're one problem at three sizes: the work of holding a shared reality together, inside one head, between two people, and across a whole group. It's the same machinery every time, and learning to see it that way is most of the battle.

There is a fourth thing on the horizon, and I'll deal with it honestly rather than breathlessly, because the breathless version is everywhere and it isn't helping. You've been told to use more AI. There's probably a number attached, a count of use cases, and nobody can quite tell you what value any of it threw off. The tools feel like clever strangers: you ask them something and get a confident, fluent answer from something that has no idea who you are or what you're trying to build. That's not a failure of intelligence. It's a failure of context. And it's interesting precisely because it's the *same* failure we've been talking about the whole time. A capable mind that can't model the room gives you the right answer in the wrong way, to the wrong person, at the wrong moment, whether that mind is silicon or a colleague who never learned to read people. I'll come to where these tools fit, much later and more modestly than the hype demands, but the point worth planting now is that they don't change the underlying problem. They just hold a mirror up to it. The shared-context problem was always there. The machines have only made it impossible to ignore.

This book is, in the end, about that shared context: how a self is built from it, how relationships maintain it, how organisations live and die by it, and what we've lost in a world that's quietly stopped doing the informal work that used to keep it alive. The thread running through all of it is peopling, the verb, the thing we are always doing with each other and rarely doing on purpose. My argument is that if you understand it as an activity rather than a mystery, you can get deliberately better at it: as a person trying to live one congruent life across all your roles, as a leader trying to build a team that actually coheres, and as an organisation trying to turn a crowd into something that moves together.

From here we go in order, from the inside out. First the self, and how a single mind manufactures the story of who it is. Then the pair, where peopling really lives, and where trust and connection get redefined as the careful maintenance of mutual models. Then the group, where shared context becomes the whole job and the modern workplace keeps dropping it. And only then, once the human picture is solid, the question of what a machine can and can't take part in.

We think, therefore I am. The rest is detail. Let's start inside one head.
