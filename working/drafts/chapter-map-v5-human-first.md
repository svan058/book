# The Context Layer — Chapter Map v5 (human-first cut)

Date: 2026-06-03
Supersedes chapter-map-v4 (the AI-heavy 8-chapter cut). Rebuilt to match the
restructured, AI-light introduction. Spine: **Self → Pair → Group → Machine**,
bookended by the problem (foreword) and the philosophical close (coda).

Guiding rule: this is a book about **peopling and shared context**, with AI as
the late, modest payoff, not the engine. Three human parts do the heavy lifting;
the machine arrives only once the human picture is solid.

## Front matter

### Foreword — Setting the Scene *(in git: 00)*
The 3am problem state, no solutions. Four faces of one problem: the incongruent
overworked self; teams without trust or alignment; shared context as 90% of the
work; AI on the horizon as KPI theatre. Lands "that's me," names the four as one
problem at four sizes. Already written and pushed.

### Introduction — We Think, Therefore I Am *(in git: 01, being restructured)*
The engine in miniature: personhood is a verb, not a thing in the skull; the self
is installed from outside; reality is a shared model held across minds. Sets the
self → pair → group → machine order. Will be split into chapters later; this map
is that split.

## Part One — The Self (inside one head)

### 1. The Self You Didn't Choose
The self is not a sealed unit behind the eyes. You get built into a person by
others being aware of you, the way you learn your own face from a mirror.
Descartes inverted: *we think, therefore I am.* The historical arc, individualism
as a recent invention hardening around the Reformation, the ancient world's more
porous, "emotions as external forces" self (Homer, the Hebrew Bible). The point:
who you are was assembled, mostly by other people, mostly without your say.

### 2. The Committee in Your Head
You are not one voice; you're a crowd of sub-personal forces that mostly stay in
sync. The brain acts first and narrates after: you're an after-the-fact observer
writing the story of why you did what you did. Anchors: the split-brain
confabulation, the L-theanine calm-standups story, the dream car-crash where
three reactions fired and none agreed. What it does to self-trust: your reasons
are often the press release, not the minutes.

### 3. One Life, Too Many Roles
The professional cost of all this: context-switching between corporate-you,
parent-you, founder-you, each a slightly different character with different
values, and the tax of holding them congruent. Busyness as anaesthetic, always
solving problems to keep the mind occupied and dodge the question of meaning.
The argument for congruency: one genuine self across the roles is a more robust,
cheaper-to-run foundation than a facade per room. (The barefoot-singer image
lives here.)

## Part Two — The Pair (between two heads)

### 4. Peopling
The flagship idea, given its own chapter. People as a verb. Recursive
self-modelling: my model of you, my model of your model of me, and on down. The
second level is the whole game of social competence; most failures are just
people stuck on the first level, answering the question and missing the room.
The tax of modelling, and why heavy modellers retreat to a few relationships.

### 5. Trust Is a Maintained Model
Trust demystified: not a feeling, but a well-maintained model of another person,
the ability to predict what they'll do when you're not in the room. Why the
dinner that never mentions the deal *is* the work. Relationships as models that
drift without maintenance: "we couldn't connect" usually means a model never got
finished. Ritual, contact, and repetition as the maintenance schedule.

### 6. The Borrowed Person
The distributed self at full strength: Hofstadter and Carol, a person as a
pattern that keeps running on hardware that isn't their own. We keep
low-resolution people alive in our heads and let them regulate us, which is half
of what reverence and religion do (what would they think of this?). Sets up,
quietly, the later question of whether a model can hold a person, without yet
making it about machines.

## Part Three — The Group (across many heads)

### 7. Most of Work Is Sync
The hidden truth of organisations: ninety percent of the week is not the work,
it's maintaining a shared picture of the work. Meetings, status, roll-ups,
alignment as context distribution. Coordination, not talent, is the scarce
thing. The army that hasn't drilled, the team where nobody knows their position.

### 8. Storming Is Personhood, Out Loud
Teams as the same machinery as relationships. Forming/storming/norming/performing
read correctly: the storm is everyone renegotiating who they are relative to
everyone else and rebuilding roles. Looks like personality clashes; it's
personhood being negotiated in real time. The project-rescue material lives here.

### 9. The Third Places We Lost
What modern work broke. We evolved to maintain shared models for free in tribes
of ~150; now we barely know our colleagues and lean on formal channels. The
distributed, cameras-off workforce, the deletion of informal contact, the
thinning of third places since Covid, and the split-personality cost of rigid
corporate identities. The cultural diagnosis at the heart of the book.

## Part Four — The Machine (modest, late)

### 10. The Clever Stranger
AI introduced as the *same* failure, not a new magic. A capable mind that can't
read the room: fluent, confident, no idea who you are, right answer wrong room.
Intelligence as a property of culture and language, not a spark inside the box,
the "it's smart because we are" point, kept light. Why current tools feel like
clever strangers and read as toys.

### 11. The Context Bridge
The modest payoff. If personhood comes from culture and is maintained through
context, the same process can partly run in a tool, not to replace people, but
to do the alignment work humans are bad at and short on time for: translating one
head into another, carrying shared context, accelerating the sync that Part Three
showed eats the week. Honest about limits and about what should stay human.
