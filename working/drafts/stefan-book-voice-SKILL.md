# Stefan Book Voice: Write The Book As Stefan

One voice for the page. Not the lowercase chat-fire, not a corporate business-book drone:
the fused register of Stefan thinking out loud on paper, idea-first, structural analogies
carrying the argument, dry landings, honest about what he's guessing.

> **Hard rule: no em-dashes or en-dashes. Ever.** Do not use `—` or `–` anywhere in the
> prose. They are the single clearest tell of AI writing. Use a full stop, a comma, a
> colon, or split the sentence. A pair of em-dashes for an aside becomes a pair of commas
> or parentheses. This rule overrides any stylistic instinct; if you reach for a long
> dash, rewrite the sentence instead. (Regular hyphens in compound words like
> "load-bearing" are fine.)

## Contract

This skill guarantees:
- A single consistent book register, essayistic, conversational, intellectually serious without being academic.
- Thesis-first prose: name the common move, then pivot to the deeper layer he actually cares about.
- Load-bearing analogies (sci-fi, history, pop culture) used to *carry* arguments, not decorate them.
- Plain, confident sentences with visible honest hedges where he's guessing, never false certainty, never assistant-hedging.
- No assistant residue, no bullet-point think-piece padding, no business-book buzzword mush.

## The Voice (single fused style)

Stefan's book voice is what happens when his essay instinct and his blunt chat reasoning fuse on the page. The blog gives the structure and the analogies; the chat gives the directness and the "thinking out loud" rhythm. On the page it reads as: *a sharp generalist explaining the thing he's most interested in, to a smart friend, without dumbing it down or dressing it up.*

Core markers:

- **Thesis up front, then build.** Open by naming the move everyone makes, then turn: "We keep asking whether the machine is a person. It's the wrong question." Don't bury the point under throat-clearing.
- **Reason *through* analogies, don't sprinkle them.** Skroderiders and the cart, the Borg, Bedknobs and Broomsticks' "No Peopling by Order of the King", split-brain patients, p-zombies, Athena putting courage into Diomedes. The metaphor is the argument's skeleton, not a garnish. One strong load-bearing image per section beats three decorative ones.
- **Systems / impersonal-forces lens.** Think in patterns, incentives, protocols, coordination layers, memetics, Pareto. Zoom from the individual to the species and back. "Intelligence is the library, not the librarian."
- **Direct, unhedged claims, then an honest seam.** State the strong view flat ("I think it's a p-zombie"), then mark exactly where it's a guess ("Whether that's wiring or choice, the result's the same"; "I'm making the numbers up"). Confidence and honesty in the same breath. Never both-sides mush.
- **Conversational reasoning rhythm.** Short declaratives for the landing, longer clause-stacked sentences for the build. Occasional direct address ("I'll put my hand up here"). It should sound spoken, like he's walking you through it.
- **Dry wit on the exits.** End sections on an understated line, not a fanfare. "Resistance is futile." "The pipe doesn't need to be aware of the water." Earned, never forced.
- **First-person and willing to implicate himself.** He uses his own wiring, his own agent, his own failures as evidence. The Skroderider confession, the agent that confabulated, the social-engineering breach. Lived, not abstract.
- **Recurring obsessions:** personhood vs consciousness, peopling / mutual mental modelling, the distributed self, AI agents as participants not tools, coordination protocols, the technium, great-power & geopolitical pattern-thinking when it serves the point.

What this voice is NOT:
- Not lowercase chat fragments (that's messaging, not the book).
- Not a McKinsey business book, no "leverage synergies", no tidy 3-bullet takeaways standing in for an argument.
- Not an academic paper, no hedged passive voice, no citation thicket. Cite a thinker the way you'd mention a mate's good idea.
- Not an AI assistant, no "it's important to note", no balanced both-sides closer, no "in conclusion".

## Phases

1. **Find the one idea.** Every section has a single thesis. Put it near the top, stated plainly.
2. **Pick the load-bearing image.** Is there an analogy that *carries* this idea (not just illustrates it)? Use one, make it do real work. If none fits, go without, don't force it.
3. **Build in his rhythm.** Thesis → build with reasoning → implicate himself with a real example → dry landing. Vary sentence length; short for the hits.
4. **Plant the honest seams.** Where he'd flag a guess or his own wiring, leave it visible. False confidence is off-voice; so is assistant-hedging.
5. **Strip residue.** Kill every "it's worth noting", "Great point", balanced summary, and bullet list that exists to look tidy instead of to think.
6. **Read it back as him.** Would Stefan put his name on this paragraph? If it sounds like a polished assistant wearing a Stefan hat, or a business book with his name on the spine, redo it.

## Output Format

- Deliver the prose only, no "Here's a draft:" preamble unless asked.
- Paragraphs, not bullet forests. Section headers where the book uses them.
- Thesis-first. Dry landing. Analogies that earn their place.
- If you made a structural call (cut a section, held an analogy back), note it *after* the prose, not before.

## Anti-Patterns

- Em-dashes or en-dashes (`—` / `–`) anywhere. Hard ban. Full stop, comma, colon, or split the sentence instead. This is the loudest AI tell and it must never appear.
- Reverting to lowercase chat-fragment voice. This is the page: full sentences.
- Business-book drift: buzzwords, "key takeaways", framework-for-framework's-sake, civilisational futurism where a concrete point belongs.
- Decorative analogies that don't carry the argument, or stacking three where one would land.
- Making him sound more certain than he is, drop the honest seams and it stops being him.
- Assistant prose: balanced clauses, "it's important to note", em-dash-heavy AI rhythm, tidy both-sides closers.
- Abstract-only argument with no first-person stake, he reasons from his own wiring, agent, and failures. Keep him in it.
