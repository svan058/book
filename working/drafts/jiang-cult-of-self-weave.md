The Jiang talk doesn't need its own section. Its job is to reinforce arguments the book already makes, with older and better-credentialed voices. Primary weave below; secondary homes and one caution noted after.

---

## PRIMARY — Chapter 2, "The self is a recent invention"
*Insert as a new paragraph after the Reformation paragraph ("...the ancients had the more accurate picture, and we are the odd ones out.") and before "## Carol, who kept running."*

And the bounded self did not go unchallenged once it arrived. The strongest objection to it is not modern at all. In the nineteenth century the anarchist Mikhail Bakunin looked at the lone individual standing free and called it a fantasy: to be free in absolute isolation, he wrote, is an absurdity, the freedom of nothingness, which is slavery. His alternative is almost word for word the claim of this book. "I am free," he wrote, "only when my individuality, reflected in the mirror of the equally free consciousness of every individual around me, comes back to me strengthened by everyone's recognition." That is we think, therefore I am, dressed as a theory of freedom. The self does not shrink when you tie it to other people. Being tied to them is the only condition under which there is a self at all. Dostoevsky put the same thing as a moral fact rather than a political one: we cannot forgive ourselves or redeem ourselves alone, we are handed back to ourselves by other people or not at all.

## SECONDARY — Chapter 3, "We used to get this for free"
*Optional. The bubble/feed/meaning material already lives here. Jiang's contribution is a name for the disease and a date. Could fold into the second paragraph (the feeds/bubbles one), or run as a short beat after it.*

There is a name for what the feeds are selling, and it is older than the feeds. The critic Jiang Xueqin calls it the cult of the self: the long cultural turn, running from modernist art through pop psychology, that taught each of us we are the source of our own meaning and answerable to nobody else for it. For most of its life it was a luxury, something only the comfortable had the time to indulge. Social media democratised it. Now everyone can spend the day tending a self for an audience, and the timing is hard to ignore: the sharp rise in reported depression among the young tracks the arrival of the smartphone almost to the year. The thing sold to us as connection turned out to be the cult of the self handed to everyone at once, and a self with no one really in the room to recognise it does not flourish. It starves.

---

## CAUTION — handle the "it was engineered" claim carefully
Jiang's most provocative thread is that the cult of the self was *deliberately* spread (the CIA-and-modernism reading: individualism promoted to inoculate the West against communism's call to collective action). It is a real and sourced argument, but contested history, and asserting it flat would cost the book credibility with exactly the skeptical reader it wants. If used at all, stage it as someone's reading rather than fact, and only in a register that can carry it: the Coda ("The Universe Trying to Wake Up"), where the impersonal-forces lens already lives, not the descriptive spine of Ch.2 or Ch.3.

## Why this placement
- **Bakunin in Ch.2** does the most work for the least risk: a 19th-century radical independently arriving at "we think, therefore I am" turns the book's central inversion from Stefan's claim into a position serious thinkers have held for over a century. It also extends the section's existing move (the bounded self is the anomaly) into "and people saw through it the whole time."
- **The cult of the self in Ch.3** gives the meaning-crisis material a name and an empirical hook (the 2015 smartphone/depression inflection) without inventing a new throughline.
- Dostoevsky and Bakunin are quotable anchors; Jiang is the synthesist who connected them, so he gets cited as the connector, not as the authority.
