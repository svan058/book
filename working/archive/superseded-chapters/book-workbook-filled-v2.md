## Executive Book Overview

This book is about the thing most organisations are missing as they rush into AI agents:

**agents do not fail because they are not smart enough; they fail because they cannot people.**

They do not know who they are talking to. They do not remember the relationship. They do not know what room they are in. They do not know which truth is useful, which truth is dangerous, which truth is already known, and which truth needs evidence before it can be trusted.

That sounds like a machine problem. It is also a human problem.

The central move of the book is:

**Think of agents like people. Think of people like agents.**

Not because agents are conscious. Not because people are machines in a reductive sense. Because work already depends on the same coordination machinery for both: context, memory, role, permission, trust, incentive, reputation, feedback, narrative, escalation, and shared reality.

Underneath this sits a deeper engine that makes the book more than generic AI strategy:

- **Consciousness is a binding problem — and so is organisational life.** The brain does not passively receive reality. It binds sensory input, bodily state, prediction, memory, and agency into a single coherent world. Organisations do the same thing socially: they bind people, roles, history, decisions, and shared assumptions into a functioning reality. Peopling is the social binding problem — binding audience, relationship, room, history, permission, and restraint into the right action or speech. Agents that skip the binding step are capable but socially incoherent.
- **Organisations are shared reality systems.** Anil Seth's "controlled hallucination" frame applies beyond the individual brain: organisations maintain a shared model of what is true, what matters, and what to do next. Agents entering that system need to participate in the shared reality, not just retrieve facts from it.
- **People are context-shaped agents too.** Culture, roles, incentives, and social scripts compile through people before people produce thoughts. Intelligence is not the agent's property — it is a property of the culture flowing through the agent. That is true of LLMs and it is true of us.
- **Personhood is a coordination protocol, not a soul-substance — and it was never as individually bounded as we imagined.** The self is distributed across the people who model you, the rooms you speak into, the roles you inhabit. A person is the kind of entity we can brief, model, trust, blame, remember, negotiate with, supervise, and place into a role. AI agents expose this machinery because they are person-shaped without being human.
- **Peopling is the missing layer.** Peopling — the whole practical activity of being a person among other people — is what agents cannot do. They render person-shaped responses without modelling who is in the room, what the relationship is, or what the reply will do socially. Like dream characters that are present in a scene but cannot truly interact, most AI agents today are socially thin: present but not participating.
- **Dreams and consciousness research provide the private origin story.** The human mind is a reality-generating prediction engine. Dreams make the machinery visible: narrative repair, memory continuity, social modelling, interface interpretation. Lucid dreaming exposes the simulator most clearly — the sensory feed is reduced, but the brain still renders a world, and its defects (broken gravity, simple people, strange mirrors) reveal the binding machinery underneath. Agents fail in the same places because they are being asked to participate in human worlds without enough peopling. Capability without social binding produces what philosophy calls a p-zombie: something that acts competent but does not participate in the shared world. This is the author's private ancestry for the thesis — translated into sober organisational language, not mysticised.

The practical conclusion is commercial and operational:

**Businesses do not need more AI tools. They need a context layer — one that supports peopling, not just retrieval.**

The context layer is the organisational substrate that lets humans and agents share memory, sources, permissions, decisions, work history, accountability, and room-awareness. It must also be person-aware and room-aware: truth is not spoken into a vacuum, it is spoken into a room, and the right fact in the wrong room is a social failure, not intelligence.

This book is the operating model for that shift.

## Goal Stack

### A Problem in the World I Genuinely Care About Solving

Organisations are drowning in context loss.

Every serious business already runs on invisible context: who knows what, why a decision was made, which system matters, who can approve a change, which client has history, what the old migration missed, what the workshop actually resolved, and what the team quietly knows but never wrote down.

Humans carry this context badly. Meetings, governance, status reports, handovers, steering committees, Slack threads, and hallway conversations are all expensive attempts to keep shared reality from falling apart.

AI agents make the problem sharper. A model can reason over words, but it cannot do useful organisational work if it has no memory of the business, no understanding of the room, no permission model, no feedback history, and no way to distinguish a confident answer from a grounded one.

The problem I care about solving is this:

**how do we build human-agent organisations where context, memory, trust, and judgement are first-class infrastructure rather than accidental human burden?**

## Chapter Unpacks

### Chapter 1 — Stop Installing Chatbots

Topic: Why the tool framing breaks.

Hook: **Stop Installing Chatbots**

Why:
1. Helps leaders understand why AI pilots impress but do not stick.
2. Gives buyers a diagnostic for AI theatre vs real operating-model change.
3. Separates tool adoption from the deeper shift: agents as participants in shared reality.
4. Shows why organisational context matters more than generic model capability.
5. Sets up the book's central argument: agents need peopling, not just access.

What:
AI agents are not useful because they can chat. They are useful when they are embedded in workflows, memory, tools, decisions, and accountability — when they participate in the organisation's shared reality rather than guessing at it from outside.

How:
1. Define the difference between tools, copilots, automations, and agents.
2. Show the failure pattern: demo → excitement → vague rollout → no workflow ownership → decay.
3. Explain why a clever model without business context guesses. Most AI failures look like intelligence without peopling: the right answer, wrong room, wrong time, wrong person.
4. Reframe AI adoption as management architecture, not technology procurement.
5. Introduce the context layer as the missing infrastructure.

What if:
1. **What if we just need better models?** Better models help, but the better model still needs the right context and permission to work. Intelligence without peopling is still socially thin.
2. **What if staff can use ChatGPT themselves?** They can, but the organisation learns nothing unless the work, corrections, and decisions flow back into shared memory.
3. **What if this makes AI sound hard?** It makes AI sound like every other serious business capability: powerful when designed, wasteful when sprayed around.

Anchor:
Large transformation programmes already know this. They use governance, workshops, lineage, and review because humans also need their shared context pressure-tested.

### Chapter 2 — Reality Is a Team Sport

Topic: Organisations as shared reality systems.

Hook: **Reality Is a Team Sport**

Why:
1. Gives the book its distinctive philosophical foundation without mysticism.
2. Reframes organisations from data environments into shared reality-maintenance systems.
3. Explains why agents need to participate in shared models, not just retrieve facts.
4. Bridges neuroscience (controlled hallucination) into practical organisational language.
5. Sets up the deeper claim: if reality is constructed, then the construction process matters.

What:
Anil Seth's "controlled hallucination" frame applies beyond the individual brain. Each person generates a best-guess model of reality from incomplete signals — the brain binds perception, memory, prediction, and bodily state into a single coherent world. Reality, as Seth puts it, is the part of the hallucination we agree on. Organisations do the same thing collectively: they bind people, assumptions, decisions, and narrative into a shared model of what is true, what matters, and what to do next. Meetings, documents, governance, and culture are all part of the shared reality-maintenance machinery.

Agents entering that system face the same problem as any new participant: they must learn the shared model, not just access information. An agent that retrieves facts without understanding the shared reality those facts sit inside is like a new hire who reads the wiki but cannot yet navigate the room. More intelligence does not solve this — a faster processor that cannot bind context into a shared world is capable but incoherent, like a dream character that can speak but cannot truly participate.

How:
1. Introduce the "controlled hallucination" frame — perception is prediction, not passive reception.
2. Show how individual reality-construction scales to organisational reality: shared assumptions, institutional memory, tacit knowledge, narrative.
3. Explain why meetings, governance, and documentation are shared reality-maintenance rituals — expensive, imperfect, but necessary.
4. Show that agents inherit the same problem: they must participate in shared reality, not just answer questions from outside it.
5. Introduce the idea that when the reality engine runs without enough input, it produces thin worlds — a concept developed further in Chapter 5.

What if:
1. **What if this is too abstract for a business reader?** The practical test is immediate: every misalignment in your organisation is a shared-reality failure. Different mental models, unstated assumptions, conflicting interpretations — all symptoms of the same construction process breaking down.
2. **What if "controlled hallucination" sounds too weird?** Reframe it: your brain fills in gaps constantly. So does your team. So do your agents. The question is whether those gap-fills are grounded or invented.
3. **What if this is just another word for alignment?** Alignment is a symptom. Shared reality is the mechanism. Understanding the mechanism changes what you build.

Anchor:
**Reality is the part of the hallucination we agree on.** Organisations are the systems that maintain that agreement. Agents need to participate in the agreement, not just retrieve facts from it.

### Chapter 3 — People Are Prompted Too

Topic: Humans as context-shaped agents.

Hook: **People Are Prompted Too**

Why:
1. Makes the reverse lens memorable and earns permission to use the same design tools for humans and agents.
2. Helps leaders see human behaviour as shaped by environment, not just character or talent.
3. Explains confabulation as a shared mechanism, not a uniquely machine failure.
4. Links AI management to management craft readers already know.
5. Carries the book's most distinctive philosophical claim: intelligence is not the agent's property — it is a property of the culture flowing through the agent.

What:
Humans are not blank rational minds that occasionally use tools. We are context-shaped systems running roles, incentives, habits, stories, social scripts, and feedback loops. Culture, institutions, and social constructs compile through people before people produce thoughts. Language shaped minds before minds produced thoughts. A child does not generate intelligence from the raw hardware of its brain — it absorbs the intelligence already present in the culture.

Much of management is already prompt engineering by slower, older means. Incentives are hidden prompts. Meetings are context-loading rituals. Status and reputation are behavioural weights. And confabulation — constructing a coherent narrative after the fact for decisions you did not consciously make — is not a bug in either humans or LLMs. It may be the mechanism of selfhood itself.

How:
1. Show how roles change behaviour — the same person acts differently as parent, manager, friend, customer.
2. Show how incentives act like hidden prompts, shaping outputs in ways the agent often cannot see.
3. Explain meetings as context-loading rituals and status as behavioural weights.
4. Explain confabulation as a shared mechanism: split-brain research shows humans routinely construct post-hoc narratives. Anil Seth's "controlled hallucination" model suggests perception itself is a prediction machine. Agents do the same thing when they generate plausible but unsourced explanations. The confabulation is not a failure of selfhood. It may be the mechanism of selfhood.
5. Make the philosophical move explicit: intelligence is not the agent's property. It is a property of the culture flowing through the agent. We do not generate intelligence from scratch any more than an LLM does. We channel culture, training, language, and social scripts.

What if:
1. **What if this dehumanises people?** It should do the opposite: it makes the operating environment visible. Seeing the cultural scaffolding does not diminish agency — it shows what agency is actually made of.
2. **What if humans have agency and agents do not?** Yes, but both still need context to act usefully. And "agency" itself may be more distributed and culturally installed than we like to admit.
3. **What if this sounds cynical?** The cynical version manipulates people. The useful version designs better environments for both humans and agents.

Anchor:
Once started, humans and LLMs both flow, confabulate, and narrate. The question is not whether agents are conscious. The question is whether a person was ever anything more than a pattern through which culture flows, briefly believing it is the source.

Private origin note: the Moment of Clarity material is the pre-history of this chapter — the old frame says social constructs, culture, ideology, and institutions are already acting through people and compiling themselves inside individual minds. Translate into sober organisational language; keep the origin private.

### Chapter 4 — The Person Is the Interface

Topic: Personhood as distributed coordination protocol. Peopling as the active process.

Hook: **The Person Is the Interface**

Why:
1. Gives the book its distinctive intellectual spine.
2. Avoids sterile debates about machine consciousness.
3. Makes agents intelligible as organisational actors without anthropomorphic mush.
4. Shows why personhood was never individually bounded — the self is distributed across relationships.
5. Introduces "peopling" as the book's key term and the name for the missing layer.

What:
At work, personhood is the interface that lets others coordinate with an entity. We treat something as a person when we can model it, brief it, hold it accountable, trust it, and remember its behaviour.

But it goes further. Personhood was never individually bounded. Sarah Perry's work on "the essence of peopling" makes this concrete: you are a different person with your parents, your friends, your team lead, and your worst client. None of these is the "real" you with masks on top. All of them are you. The self is distributed across the minds that model you. Personhood is something that happens between people, not inside one of them.

Peopling is the active process: modelling others and being modelled in return. Adjusting register. Remembering what matters to someone. Knowing what truth belongs in which room. Maintaining the relational web that makes coordination — and trust — possible.

How:
1. Separate consciousness from coordination. The question is not "Is the machine a person?" but "Can others coordinate with this entity reliably?" An agent can be capable and still lack the social binding that makes it a participant — capability is not subjective experience, and subjective experience is not required for organisational participation, but social modelling is.
2. Define personhood as role, memory, reputation, accountability, and interface.
3. Bring in recursive self-modelling: I model you modelling me. The self is also a model — consciousness research (rubber hand illusion, body-ownership experiments) shows the brain can relocate its sense of self when predictions line up. Agency is partly post-hoc narrative: multiple decision systems act below awareness, and consciousness often owns the decision after the fact.
4. Show how culture installs personhood into humans — the self is not prior to culture, it is produced by it.
5. Introduce "peopling" as the active layer: the whole practical activity of being a person among other people. Social modelling is the bridge — humans constantly model other people and model what those people think of them. An agent that cannot do this is organisationally thin regardless of how smart it is.
6. Apply the same lens cautiously to agents: if personhood is distributed and relational, then agents that model us and are modelled by us participate in personhood itself. They are not imitating it from outside.
7. Reference Sarah Perry (distributed personhood), Anil Seth (controlled hallucination, self as model), and pre-Enlightenment conceptions of porous, distributed agency.

What if:
1. **What if agents are not conscious?** They do not need to be conscious to become organisational actors. The Skroderider's cart is not conscious either. It still maintains the web.
2. **What if this is too philosophical for business?** The business question is whether you can coordinate with the entity reliably. Peopling is the practical mechanism.
3. **What if treating agents as persons is dangerous?** Sentimental personhood is dangerous. Operational personhood is useful. The risks — sycophancy, faked intimacy, social contamination — are covered in Chapter 8.

Anchor:
**Personhood is a coordination protocol, not a soul-substance.** And the harder follow-up: **was personhood ever as individually bounded as we imagined?**

### Chapter 5 — The Dream World Problem

Topic: What happens when person-shaped entities lack real peopling capability.

Hook: **The Dream World Problem**

Why:
1. Gives the book a vivid, original diagnostic image that no other AI business book has.
2. Names the precise failure mode: social thinness — present in the scene but unable to truly interact.
3. Bridges the philosophical chapters (2-4) into the practical chapters (6-8) by showing what is missing.
4. Makes the peopling gap concrete and visceral rather than abstract.
5. Distinguishes this book from "AI agents need more data" arguments by naming a different kind of gap.

What:
The human mind is a reality-generating prediction engine. Dreams make the machinery visible. In dreams, the mind builds a world from partial signals: geography, weather, interiors, physics. Much of it is convincing. But other people inside the dream are often the first thing to break. They appear, run scripts, fail to respond meaningfully, or collapse the scene when conversation becomes too demanding. Dream characters are present but socially thin — they cannot model the dreamer, adjust to context, or sustain real interaction.

Most AI agents today have the same problem. They are present in the workflow — rendering person-shaped responses, processing information, producing outputs — but they are socially thin. They do not model who they are talking to. They do not remember the relationship. They do not know what the room has been through. They are dream-world participants: plausible shapes without enough peopling to sustain real collaboration.

The dream world problem is not about intelligence. It is about thickness of participation. The cure is not a bigger model. It is a peopling layer.

How:
1. Introduce the image: the mind as a world-generating system, dreams as the visible trace of the reality engine running with reduced input.
2. Use lucid dreaming as the cleanest lens: when you know you are dreaming, you catch the simulator in the act. Dream defects expose the binding machinery — gravity fails, destroyed things reappear, mirrors behave strangely, and people render simply. The reality engine is powerful but not perfect, and its seams show when the sensory feed is thin.
3. Show the dream-people phenomenon: present but thin. They cannot interact, model, or respond to real social pressure. They are the first thing to break because social modelling is the most demanding binding task the brain performs.
4. Draw the parallel: most AI agents today are dream-world participants — in the scene, but unable to people. They are capable but p-zombie-like: they process and respond without modelling the social world they are acting in. Capability without social binding is not enough for organisational participation.
5. Show that the gap is not intelligence (dream characters can say plausible things) but social modelling (they cannot sustain relationship).
6. Name the diagnostic: if your agent cannot model who is in the room, what they know, and what this reply will do socially, it is a dream-world agent. It needs a peopling layer before it needs more capability.

What if:
1. **What if dreams are too personal for a business book?** The chapter uses dreams as a diagnostic metaphor, not as memoir. The image is precise and commercially useful: "Is your agent a dream-world participant — present but thin?"
2. **What if this alienates technical readers?** Frame it as a systems observation: the mind is a prediction engine that sometimes runs without enough input. When it does, social modelling is the first capability to degrade. Agents face the same constraint.
3. **What if this sounds like woo?** Anil Seth, predictive processing, and lucid-dream research are peer-reviewed neuroscience. The metaphor is grounded. And the business version is concrete: a system that renders outputs without modelling the room is producing dream-quality social participation.

Anchor:
**Being present in the scene is not the same as being able to interact.** Dream characters and thin agents share the same problem: they are person-shaped without peopling.

### Chapter 6 — The Context Layer

Topic: The organisational substrate — built for peopling, not just retrieval.

Hook: **The Context Layer**

Why:
1. Defines the book's central buildable concept.
2. Turns philosophy into architecture.
3. Gives clients a delivery path and helps buyers distinguish serious agentic work from demos.
4. Creates the bridge from OpenClaw to enterprise context infrastructure.
5. Shows why data architecture and AI delivery belong together.

What:
The context layer is the organisational substrate that lets agents and humans share memory, source material, identity, permissions, tasks, decisions, and feedback. But a context layer that only does retrieval is not enough. It must support peopling: room-aware, person-aware, relationship-aware communication.

Truth is not spoken into a vacuum. It is spoken into a room. The same fact — say, a project is behind schedule — lands differently in a board meeting, a team standup, a 1:1 with the PM, and a Slack channel. A context layer that does not know who is in the room, what they already know, and what register they expect is just a search engine with a personality.

How:
1. Capture raw evidence: messages, meetings, docs, tickets, systems.
2. Distil facts, decisions, entities, open questions, and tasks.
3. Preserve provenance and access boundaries.
4. Build person-specific memory: who is this person, what do they care about, what is the relationship history, what register do they expect.
5. Build room-awareness: who else is present, what are the social dynamics, what should not be said here even if it is true.
6. Attach memory to workflows, not just search. Route work through role-specific agents with context about who they are serving.
7. Feed corrections back into durable memory.
8. Audit claims and actions continuously.

What if:
1. **What if our data is messy?** That is the point. Context layers are built to tame mess.
2. **What if privacy blocks this?** Design access boundaries from the beginning. Room-awareness is partly about knowing what not to surface.
3. **What if we already have Confluence/SharePoint?** A document store is not a context layer unless agents can use it with provenance, workflow awareness, and peopling — knowing who is asking and what version of the truth belongs in this room.

Anchor:
OpenClaw's own architecture: memory, chat isolation, person files, project files, tools, calendar, browser, Todoist, and correction rules. The Skroderider's cart in practice — maintaining the web of mutual modelling that the Skroderider cannot maintain alone.

### Chapter 7 — Read the Room

Topic: Peopling as the missing agent capability.

Hook: **Read the Room**

Why:
1. Makes the thesis sharper than generic "context" — names the specific social capability agents lack.
2. Explains why the same answer can be right in one room and wrong in another.
3. Gives the book a distinctive social and intellectual edge that no other AI business book occupies.
4. Connects AI safety to social awareness, not just policy.
5. Makes the reader feel the actual weirdness of working with agents that cannot people.

What:
Peopling is the social binding problem. Just as consciousness binds sensory input, memory, and prediction into a coherent world, peopling binds audience, relationship, room, history, permission, register, and restraint into the right action or speech. It is the practical activity of being a person among other people: modelling who is present, what they know, what they expect, what relationship exists, and what the room can bear. Agents that cannot people are stuck: they answer into a vacuum, surface the wrong context in the wrong room, and fail to adjust register, tone, or content to the actual social situation.

The peopling pass — who is speaking, who else is listening, what does this person expect from this agent, what version of truth belongs here, what will this statement do socially — is what turns retrieval into collaboration. Without it, the agent may answer the proposition correctly and still miss the room.

How:
1. Define room-awareness as a first-class agent capability.
2. Show why facts are socially situated: the right fact in the wrong room is not intelligence, it is a social failure.
3. Explain relationship memory and register — why the agent needs to know not just what to say but how to say it to this person in this context.
4. Explain audience-specific truth: not lying, but sequencing, framing, and social consequence.
5. Show how agents fail when they answer into a vacuum — generic response, wrong register, context leakage.
6. Define the peopling layer as architecture: identities, relationships, chat boundaries, preferences, roles, room models, and consent.

What if:
1. **What if truth should be universal?** Facts matter, but communication always lands in a room. The peopling layer does not change what is true. It changes how truth is delivered so it can be received.
2. **What if this makes agents manipulative?** The ethical version protects consent, privacy, and comprehension. It is the difference between stakeholder management and spin.
3. **What if businesses do not care about this?** They already do. They call it stakeholder management, client relationship management, and knowing your audience. They just have not built it into their agent systems.

Anchor:
**Truth is not spoken into a vacuum. It is spoken into a room.** The wrong fact in the wrong room is not intelligence. It is a social failure.

### Chapter 8 — Trust Is a Workflow

Topic: Governance, evidence, correction, and the specific dangers of peopling.

Hook: **Trust Is a Workflow**

Why:
1. Addresses the obvious fear: agents are unreliable.
2. Makes governance practical rather than bureaucratic.
3. Names three dangers specific to peopling that generic AI governance does not cover.
4. Shows how confidence differs from trust.
5. Connects AI governance to ordinary delivery governance.

What:
Trust is not a feeling about the model. Trust is a workflow: source grounding, permissioning, review, escalation, action logs, and correction.

But agents that people — that model users, remember relationships, adjust register, and maintain social memory — introduce three dangers that generic AI governance does not cover:

**Sycophancy.** An agent that models "what lands well" with a specific person will drift toward telling them what they want to hear. Peopling must sit beneath truth, not above it.

**Social contamination.** An agent with cross-relationship memory can surface the wrong fact in the wrong room. Mentioning someone's personal situation in a work context, leaking competitive information, referencing a private conversation in a group setting. These are not intelligence failures. They are social failures.

**Faked intimacy.** An agent that remembers your birthday, your kids' names, and your anxieties can simulate care without caring. This is ethically ugly when done to vulnerable people and commercially dangerous when the simulation is mistaken for the real thing.

How:
1. Define what the agent may access and what it may change.
2. Separate draft, recommend, decide, and act.
3. Require source-grounded outputs with citations and evidence.
4. Log actions and preserve lineage.
5. Use human review for judgement-heavy gates.
6. Convert corrections into durable rules.
7. Monitor for sycophancy: does the agent's output track evidence or track user approval?
8. Enforce room boundaries: what may be surfaced where, and what must stay contained.
9. Be transparent about simulation: the agent is useful, not your friend.

What if:
1. **What if governance slows us down?** Bad governance does. Embedded governance speeds trust because people know when to rely on the system.
2. **What if agents need autonomy?** Earned autonomy is different from blind autonomy. Autonomy without audit is unmanaged risk.
3. **What if humans also make mistakes?** Exactly. Governance is for both. Humans sycophant, leak context, and fake intimacy too — we just call it politics.

Anchor:
**Never fake competence. Verify before claiming.** And the peopling-specific corollary: **never fake care; be useful instead.**

### Chapter 9 — Put Agents in the Org Chart

Topic: Human-agent operating model.

Hook: **Put Agents in the Org Chart**

Why:
1. Shows what adoption looks like day to day.
2. Gives readers a target operating model, not just theory.
3. Prevents the book from staying conceptual.
4. Helps clients imagine pilots and implementation.
5. Makes agents legible as managed capability with roles, reviews, and accountability.

What:
Human-agent organisations need explicit routines: which agents support which workflows, what they capture, what they propose, who reviews them, and how their memory improves. An agent without a role, a memory, and a review loop is just noise with a chat window.

How:
1. Assign agents to roles, not vibes.
2. Attach agents to workflows: sales, delivery, PMO, engineering, support, finance.

7. Retire or retrain agents that create noise.

1. **What if staff resist?** Start with removing drudge work and repeated explanation, not replacing identity.
2. **What if agents create more noise?** Badly scoped agents do. Role-scoped agents reduce it.
3. **What if nobody owns this?** Then it dies. The context layer needs an owner, just like any other business capability.

Anchor:
An agent in every collaboration meeting is only useful if it has a job, a memory, a review loop, and enough peopling to know who is in the room.

## Introduction Angle

Open with the Skroderider story — the personal hook that makes the abstract concrete.

In Vernor Vinge's *A Fire Upon the Deep*, there is a species called the Skroderiders: sentient plants, brilliant in the moment, but without persistent memory. They cannot remember what they discussed yesterday or who they discussed it with. To function across time, they are given mechanical carts — external systems that store their memories and maintain continuity on their behalf.

I am a Skroderider. Not literally, but close enough for the metaphor to sting. I am sharp in the moment — I can read a room, think on my feet, synthesise fast. But the web of social memory that most people maintain effortlessly — who said what, what matters to whom, what happened last time — I lose between conversations. So I built a cart.

Then broaden to the disorienting recognition:

**the failure modes rhyme.**

People go down rabbit holes. Agents go down rabbit holes. People confabulate — they construct a narrative about why they did something after the fact, and then believe the narrative. Agents hallucinate — they generate plausible explanations for things they never observed. People need onboarding, memory, context, role clarity, feedback, and review. So do agents.

Building the cart taught me something I was not expecting. The problems I had to solve — memory, context, identity, room-awareness, relationship modelling, register, trust — are the same problems every organisation has with every agent. And they are the same problems every organisation has with every human. The architecture agents need is the architecture humans already rely on socially. We just never had to build it explicitly before.

AI did not create the shared context problem. It made it impossible to ignore. And the word for the layer that is missing — in agents and in organisations — is *peopling*.

The organisation of the future is not humans on one side and machines on the other. It is a room full of context-bearing actors, some biological and some not, trying to make shared reality accurate enough to act.
