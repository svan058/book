# Perry, "The Essence of Peopling" — additional depth not yet in the book
**Date:** 2026-06-18
**Source:** Sarah Perry, *The Essence of Peopling*, ribbonfarm (2015). https://ribbonfarm.com/2015/04/08/the-essence-of-peopling/
**Why:** the book already draws the spine from this essay (people-as-a-verb, we-think-therefore-I-am, distributed self, status/modelling-budget, the skinsuit, recursive modelling). This captures what we're leaving on the table. Quotes are verbatim.

---

## The big one we're missing: WHY peopling hurts (the engine of self-consciousness)

The book explains that the self is built by others, but never gives the *mechanism* for why that's painful. Perry does, and it's the missing causal core.

**The first-person / third-person dissonance, which self-amplifies.**
> "Information about the self from the first-person perspective tends to be inflated and self-aggrandizing; information about the self from the third-person perspective, projected into the minds of others, tends to be deflated and self-deprecatory. Deep down, each person feels himself to be special and important, but also realizes that from the perspective of others he is quite ordinary and unimportant."

> [Rochat] "A main property of this dissonance is that it tends to feed into itself and can reach overwhelming proportions in the life of individuals... expressed in the nuisance of self-conscious behaviors that hinder creativity and the smooth 'flow' of interpersonal exchanges."

> "Having *others in mind* is the essential nature of peopling, but it is often quite painful, manifesting in self-conscious rumination."

*Where it goes:* Chapter 1 / Chapter 2 (the cost of recursion, the "tax"), and especially Chapter 5 (the congruence tax, why collapsing the selves is a relief). This is the deep reason the tax exists: two irreconcilable self-images, inflamed by attention. The strongest single thing to add.

## Proof by pathology: study peopling through its breakdowns

Perry's most original method, and the book has none of it.
> "All this is to say that a huge portion of our internal cognitive machinery, of which we are not normally aware, is concerned with the ordinary function of maintaining one's own identity and that of others. We can see this function from the ways in which it breaks."

The breakages name the machinery: Cotard (belief you don't exist), Capgras (loved one replaced by impostor), somatoparaphrenia (a limb isn't yours), mirrored-self misidentification, Fregoli/"phantom companion" delusions, and "ideas of reference" ("seeing reference to the self everywhere, in traffic lights and television advertisements"). Grandeur and persecution are "misperceptions of status and relationships."

*Where it goes:* Chapter 1 ("the committee," "there is nobody in there to find") and Chapter 2. A memorable evidentiary move: you can see how much cognition runs the person-machinery by watching what specific delusions appear when it fails.

## Consciousness may exist *for* peopling

Raises peopling from an activity to the purpose of conscious thought.
> "Baumeister and Masicampo posit that interfacing between identities - both within a single mind, and between minds - is the *purpose* of conscious thought."

> [Kevin Simler] we "infect" each other with personhood.

*Where it goes:* Chapter 1 and Chapter 4 (the smart cultural ghost). Strengthens "we think, therefore I am" into a claim about what consciousness is *for*. "Infect each other with personhood" is a vivid line.

---

## The stakes of being unmodelled: transparency vs substantiality

The book frames the cost of isolation as lost trust/sync; Perry adds the existential version, the dread of being *unseen*.
> "Finally one experiences the peace of being effortlessly recognized by others, the absolute sense of being socially substantial, as opposed to socially transparent... This kind of small village experience lifts the curse of social transparency."

Names impostor dread directly ("panic fear... of being an impostor").

*Where it goes:* Chapter 3 ("We used to get this for free") and Chapter 5. The transparent/substantial pair is a clean, usable frame for loneliness distinct from status rivalry.

---

## Ritual is a second channel: costly, hard-to-fake signal

The book treats sync as language/meetings. Perry adds ritual as a distinct apparatus.
> "Ritual allows for the communication of information that language can't convey - hard-to-fake costly signals of commitment, dependability, harmoniousness, and cooperative intent."

*Where it goes:* Chapter 6 ("the stuff that looks like a waste of time is the work") and Chapter 3. Gives the costly-signalling reason rituals/offsites/standups aren't waste.

---

## Modern environments starve and deform peopling

Three linked claims the book gestures at but Perry sharpens into mechanism. Strong fit for Chapter 3's "We used to get this for free."

**Mismatch / deprivation:**
> "If humans are somehow calibrated to expect a constant flow of social information, then the sparseness of ritual and social participation in modern environments might trigger a cascade of rumination."

**Ugliness turns us inward:**
> "And ugliness causes us to turn inward." / "A utilitarian 'housing unit' is a box for storing people; it doesn't have to be beautiful, just cheap."

**Introversion reframed as environmental sensitivity:**
> "Perhaps many of us who identify as introverts are just especially sensitive to the ugliness and awkwardness of modern built and social environments."

**The temporal cost (we live in simulations):**
> "Since the interface is performed using *simulations* when social information is not available, we are spread out in time, living both in the past and the future as well as the present."

*Where it goes:* Chapter 3 (the mismatch, the box-for-storing-people image), Chapter 5 (the temporal/"live in the moment" angle, the introversion reframe pairs with the existing social-exhaustion passage).

## The politics of asymmetric modelling: gods, celebrities, dictators, egregores

The book has the skinsuit and the religion-as-shared-context material; Perry has a whole richer taxonomy of one-way modelling.
> "In very complex societies, there are some people who are so high in status that it is obligatory to mentally model them, even though they do not reciprocate by mentally modeling their social inferiors."

Gods fit this better than celebrities; dictators (cults of personality) are the degenerate case, working by "preference falsification" that "prevents coordination." The egregore:
> "an autonomous psychic entity made up of, and influencing, the thoughts of a group of people... We will have gods or pseudo-gods no matter what; that is part of our inherent psychology."

**Public shaming = being forced into an egregore:**
> "If we are publicly shamed, we are obligated to suffer by modeling the masses of people with enmity toward us, even though they do not think of us as full human beings... It is a constant danger."

*Where it goes:* Chapter 3 (status/the egregore "company is a shared hallucination") and Chapter 6 (who you put in the room). Public shaming is a sharp, contemporary set-piece for the bubble/feed material in Chapter 3.

## The normative backbone: pattern languages and the wrong unit

This is the part the book's "solving" chapters could lean on, and currently don't. It's also exactly Pascal's point-2 territory (what good peopling infrastructure looks like).

**The individual is the wrong unit:**
> "The individual is not the appropriate unit of peopling, but it is the only unit that the tiling systems understand."

**Christopher Alexander / pattern languages, distance kills fit:**
> "The more the process of production is distanced by *mediation* from the use of the product, the worse it will *fit*."

> "Top-down control destroys the peopling interface in which pattern languages are used, and by which people together express their agency... Exerting more top-down control is exactly the wrong response."

*Where it goes:* Chapter 6 and Chapter 8. The normative principle: good peopling is ground-up, living, constantly maintained; top-down control is the wrong lever. This is a serious intellectual frame for the "what to do" half of the book.

---

## The payoff: a framework for meaning (and the closing image)

Perry ends on meaning, via Baumeister's "existential shopping list" (purpose, justification, efficacy, self-worth), all of which modernity dumped onto the isolated self, then adds the underlying one:
> "The peopling interface... suggests a fifth, or even underlying fundamental, need for meaning: the need to interact with others, to simulate them and be simulated by them in positive ways, no matter how mundane."

Vonnegut, repurposed: we're here to "fart around" → "fart around *together*." And the closing recursive-attention image:
> "drink tea, together with your friends; pay attention to the tea, and to your friends, and pay attention to your friends paying attention to the tea. Therein lies the meaning of life."

*Where it goes:* Chapter 5 (meaning / a congruent self needs something to cohere around) and the book's close. The tea image is recursive attention, the same structure as recursive modelling, so it pays off the whole thesis as a way to live. Strong candidate to echo near the end.
