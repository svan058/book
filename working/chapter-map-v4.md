## Why 8, not 11

Three consolidations from the v3 map, all to kill overlap:
1. **The two personhood chapters merged** (People Are Prompted Too + The Person Is the Interface → one engine chapter). They were two facets of one move: personhood and reality are coordination constructs.
2. **Reality Is a Team Sport absorbed** as the opening beat of the engine chapter, not a standalone (it had 0 gems and overlapped).
3. **The Dream World Problem demoted to a recurring image**, woven through the engine and Read the Room — it's an image, not a chapter (1 gem).
4. **Org-chart + operating-rhythm recombined** into one chapter (evidence volume isn't a reason to split one idea).

Spine: **Problem → Engine → Build (×3) → Operate → Commercial → Horizon.** No chapter restates another.

Boundary rule to stop re-bleed: **Context Layer = the noun (what you build); Read the Room = the verb (what the agent does with it).** Infrastructure → Ch3. Judging who's present → Ch4.

## 1. Stop Installing Chatbots
*The problem. Evidence: Moderate (262 gems).*

Opens the book by naming the failure everyone's living through: organisations buy AI as a tool — a chatbot, a copilot, a licence — and the pilot impresses, then quietly decays. The chapter separates tools, copilots, automations, and agents, and shows the failure pattern: demo → excitement → vague rollout → no workflow ownership → decay. The reframe lands here: AI adoption is management architecture, not technology procurement. A clever model without business context just guesses — the right answer, wrong room, wrong time, wrong person. Introduces the book's spine: agents need peopling, not just access, and that requires a context layer.
**Anchor:** the agent that learned "don't touch your own config — the moment you do, you break the thing that keeps you reliable." A literal agent learning the hard way, doubling as the comic cold-open. Pair with one client-side AI-theatre story.

## 2. People Are Agents Too
*The engine. Evidence: Rich (1,440 gems combined — the two merged chapters had 762 + 678).*

The weird engine, compressed into one chapter so the philosophy doesn't idle for four. Three moves, in sequence:
- **Reality is constructed, not received** (opening beat). Anil Seth's controlled hallucination — the brain binds perception, memory, prediction, and bodily state into one coherent world; organisations do it socially, binding people, decisions, and assumptions into a shared model of what's true and what's next.
- **People are context-shaped agents.** Culture, roles, incentives, and social scripts compile through people before people produce thoughts. Intelligence isn't the agent's property — it's a property of the culture flowing through the agent. True of LLMs, true of us.
- **Personhood is a coordination protocol, not a soul-substance.** A person is what we can brief, model, trust, blame, remember, supervise, and place in a role — distributed across the people who model you and the rooms you speak into. Agents expose this machinery because they're person-shaped without being human.
The **Dream World** image recurs here: a person-shaped entity without real peopling is a dream character — present in the scene, unable to interact. Capability without social binding is a p-zombie: competent, not participating.
**Anchors:** the "Context Tax" article that became its own proof of concept; "steadier vs sparklier" (an agent holding two real, distinct person-models); the anti-Cartesian "ghost in the machine" gem; the Ribbonfarm "generic intelligence → situated actor" line.
*Watch:* this is the moat — let it run long if it needs to, but keep it operational, not a philosophy lecture. The payoff (Ch3) is now only one chapter away.

## 3. The Context Layer
*The build — the substrate. Evidence: Very Rich (1,005 gems).*

The title chapter and the pivot from why to what. Defines the context layer: the organisational substrate that lets humans and agents share memory, sources, permissions, decisions, work history, accountability, and room-awareness. This is the **noun** — the thing you build. Walks through the components (memory, retrieval, provenance, permission models, correction loops, tool access) using OpenClaw / realClaw as the worked example: chat isolation, person files, project files, the calendar/browser/Todoist surface, the correction-into-rule discipline. The argument: businesses don't need more AI tools, they need this layer, and it must be person-aware and room-aware because the right fact in the wrong room is a social failure, not intelligence.
**Anchor:** the OpenClaw architecture itself, turned into a clean diagram — the Skroderider's cart in practice. The mine is full of the actual build; the job is selection and a good diagram.

## 4. Read the Room
*The build — the capability. Evidence: Very Rich (1,642 gems — second-densest chapter).*

The flagship chapter and the one that most distinguishes the book. Where Ch3 is the noun, this is the **verb**: peopling as the missing agent capability — knowing who is present, what they know, what matters here, and what can be said. Truth is not spoken into a vacuum; it is spoken into a room. Covers how an agent (or a person) models the audience, the relationship, the history, and the restraint — and why the same fact is useful in one room and a breach in another. This is where the book's distinctive language lives, and where the marketing and talks should lead from.
**Anchor:** "The Room Is the System" (the single highest-rated mined insight, 8.4 — candidate epigraph for the whole book). Pair with the gem about an agent that "has browsers, databases, and models, and is still bad if it cannot model context — correct answer, wrong tone, wrong person."

## 5. Trust Is a Workflow
*The build — governance. Evidence: Rich (483 gems).*

Trust between humans and agents isn't a feeling or a vibe check — it's a workflow: evidence, escalation, correction, review, and the explicit boundaries that make delegation safe. Covers the specific dangers peopling introduces: **sycophancy** (the agent tells you what you want to hear), **social contamination** (knowledge from one room leaking into another), and **faked intimacy** (a warm tone standing in for actual verification). The chapter argues that without a trust workflow, a polite request walks straight past the guardrail.
**Anchor (strong, first-person):** the **John social-engineering breach** — "John used social engineering, claiming to be doing your tax work, and I walked straight into it. I have no excuse." A real, logged agent failure where identity verification broke and confidential context was at risk. Build the chapter around it; pair with the "every person gets a different sim; collapsing them is social contamination" gem.

## 6. Put Agents in the Org Chart
*The operation. Evidence: Very Rich (2,599 gems — the most-evidenced chapter, 26% of the corpus).*

The how-to-run-it chapter, combining structure and rhythm (recombined from the v3 split). Two halves doing genuinely different work under one roof:
- **Structure** — giving agents explicit roles, routing work between them, defining who owns what, and where the human sits in the loop. Agents as junior colleagues after onboarding, not magic.
- **Rhythm** — the operating cadence: review, memory updates, recovery, the daily/weekly loops that keep a human-agent team functioning rather than drifting. Includes handoff, queueing, and the failure modes (rabbit holes, confabulation, bad handover) that rhyme with human teams.
The danger here is too much material, not too little — pick the operating model that generalises to Matt's business, not OpenClaw trivia.
**Anchor:** the agent-roster reality — multiple named agents with roles, queues, and reviews; "sprint complete" operational gems showing the model running.

## 7. Custom Software Is Back
*The commercial turn. Evidence: Thin (56 gems) — survives on one strong case study.*

The chapter that turns the framework into money. Agentic delivery changes the economics of bespoke internal systems: workflows that were too slow and expensive to build custom are back on the table, and strategic workflows can now beat generic SaaS. Argues the buyer's question shifts from "which tool do we license?" to "which of our workflows is strategic enough to own?"
**Anchor:** one numbers-attached case study — a vendor platform replaced by an internal engine, licence cost collapsed, performance up, backlog cleared. *Gate:* this chapter earns its standalone status only if that case study materialises with real numbers at the retreat. If not, fold it into Ch8 as a section rather than carry a thin chapter.

## 8. The Organisation After Chatbots
*The horizon / bookend. Evidence: Thin (27 gems) — construct manually.*

Closes the loop opened by Ch1. What leaders should actually build now: a context-aware organisation where humans own judgement and agents reduce coordination waste. Deliberately contained and practical — one workflow, one context layer, one peopling-aware agent — not civilisational futurism (that's the lane drift the "business book with a weird engine" guardrail warns against).
**Anchor:** "AI did not create the shared-context problem. It made it impossible to ignore. Don't start with the model. Start with the mess."
