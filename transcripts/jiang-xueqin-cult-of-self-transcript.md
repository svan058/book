The main influence is modernism, a transformative art movement beginning around the early 20th century.

Arguably the first great modern artist is James Joyce, who in 1922 published Ulysses. James Joyce was Irish—he was an Irish expatriate and he actually studied Dante in university. He wrote Ulysses as a way to imitate, almost surpass Dante. And of course Ulysses refers to Homer's Odyssey.

We're going to read a passage from Ulysses to understand the power of his writing:

> "Ineluctable modality of the visible: that at least if no more, through my eyes. Signatures of all things I am here to read: seaspawn and seawrack, the nearing tide, that rusty boot. Snotgreen, bluesilver, rust: coloured signs. Limits of the diaphane, but he adds: in bodies. Then he was aware of them bodies before of them coloured."

What does this mean? I have no idea. I can explain Dante to you. I can explain Homer and Shakespeare, but I struggle with James Joyce. And there are two reasons why.

**First**, he was a singer. You have to read what he writes as though it's music—it's meant to be read out loud. It's musical and that's really the power of his writing. He's more focusing on the style rather than the substance.

**Second**, he was extremely well-read and in every sentence there are multiple allusions and references to other books. You must have read what he read. You must have experienced what he experienced in order to understand him.

There are those who argue that Ulysses is the greatest book in the world. In fact, if you Google "the best book ever written in human history," James Joyce is up there. Ulysses is either number one or number two on lists of the 50 best books in human history. It's almost like doing a jigsaw puzzle.

And that's all true. But think about what they're saying. They're really saying that James Joyce believed he was God. He had the mind of God. That's very different from Dante, which was trying to use poetry to bring people into the mind of God, which is the truth of the world. Dante is a lot more accessible than James Joyce.

### Modern Literature vs. Traditional Literature

**Modern literature** as represented by Ulysses: it is elitist, self-referential, full of allusions and references but you don't actually know what the meaning is—what is the bigger story here? It's usually stream of consciousness writing, trying to capture the mind as it thinks and works.

**Traditional literature** (Homer): democratic, trying to bring beauty and truth to the people through epic poetry.

Starting with modern literature, we have this abrupt change in the nature of literature. Before, it was about empowering people to seek the truth for themselves. Now, modern literature is really just this very elite club of very arrogant, haughty people.

### Virginia Woolf and Stream of Consciousness

James Joyce was good friends with Virginia Woolf. In fact, Virginia Woolf actually published James Joyce. In 1927, Woolf published *To the Lighthouse*, probably her most famous work. In it she's also trying to respond to Joyce. *To the Lighthouse* is very much based on Homer's Odyssey and it's extremely well written.

Let's look at what she writes:

> "There were the eternal problems: suffering, death, the poor. There was always a woman dying of cancer even here. And yet she had said to all these children, 'You shall go through with it.' To eight people she had said that relentlessly. And the bill for the greenhouse would be fifty pounds."

What she's doing is reading a book and thinking about the issues raised by the book, but she's also thinking about life—like, "oh, I have to go and do something." That's really how our minds work. This captures stream of conscious thinking really well and she's heavily influenced by Freud—trying to go into the unconscious and figure out how the conscious/unconscious works. *The Lighthouse* is really about memory, about perception, about remembering.

But again, it's extremely self-indulgent and inward-looking—a radical departure from traditional literature.

### Dostoevsky vs. Modern Literature

**For Dostoevsky:**

**For modern literature:**
- Self-discovery will allow for self-mastery
- Our psychology responds to suppressed memories

It's too optimistic, too positive. It's saying: "Hey, if you're poor, don't worry about it. As long as you think happy thoughts, you'll be good." This is the idea of positive psychology that we have today.

Carl Jung and Phil Simmons also had a major influence on Pablo Picasso and you can see it from his painting *Head of a Woman*. ### Why the Cult of the Self Is Bad: Bakunin

This is Mikhail Bakunin and he explains it very well in his writings. Let's read what he wrote:

> "Having human man and freedom above all is a product of social collective labor. To be free in absolute isolation is absurdity invented by theologians, metaphysicians who have replaced the society of humans by that of God, that phantom. To say that each person feels free in the presence of God, that is the presence of absolute emptiness, nothingness. Freedom in isolation then is a freedom of nothingness or indeed the nothingness of freedom: slavery. God, the figment of God has been historically the moral source, or rather the immoral source, of all slaveries."

What he's saying: the radical turning point in human history is the invention of Christianity because it gives us the idea of individualism. We think that's a good thing because we're taught that individuality means free choice, means freedom. What he's saying is that's an absurdity. We only have freedom **from** our community. We only have freedom if others are free around us. If we are free but no one else is free, then we are slaves as well.

Because individuality prevents us from working with others, from loving others, then that makes us slaves to ourselves—and that allows the powers that be in society to better control us. He's saying Christianity is a slave religion. It was designed to make us all into slaves.

Bakunin lived in the 19th century. But if he read Freud, he would also argue that the cult of psychoanalysis is really about entrapping yourself in your own emotions.

> "As for us, we want neither phantoms nor nothingness, but living human reality. And we recognize that man can feel free, be free, and therefore can achieve freedom. In order to be free, I need to see myself surrounded by men, by free men, and be recognized as such by them. I am free only when my individuality reflected in the mirror of the equally free consciousness of every individual around me comes back to me strengthened by everyone's recognition."

What he's saying is this: if you want to be happy, if you want to be free—care about others, be kind to others, work with other people, sacrifice your own self-interest for the greater good. That is what will make you really happy.

And that's generally true. Think about this: if you're by yourself, will you be happy? Probably not. But if you have a family, you have kids—you don't have any freedom, but you're a happier person in many ways. You're a more free person because you have better control of your emotions. You have more purpose in life.

## Key Themes for the Book

### Direct Relevance to "The Context Layer"

1. **Personhood is collaborative, not individual** — Jiang's thesis aligns perfectly with Sarah Perry's inversion: "we think, therefore I am." The cult of the self isolates us; personhood emerges through relationship.

2. **Modern literature as the breakdown of peopling** — Joyce, Woolf, Picasso: art that requires you to have read what they've read, seen what they've seen. No bridge, no context layer. Just elite self-reference. Contrast with Homer/Dante: bringing truth *to* the people.

3. **Dostoevsky's peopling vs. Freud's isolation** — "We must surrender ourselves to others to seek salvation and redemption. We cannot rely on ourselves to forgive ourselves, to love ourselves." This is pure peopling. Modern psychology's turn inward: self-discovery = self-mastery. Too optimistic. Too isolating.

4. **Bakunin's freedom-through-community** — "I am free only when my individuality reflected in the mirror of the equally free consciousness of every individual around me comes back to me strengthened by everyone's recognition." This is the distributed self. Freedom is not isolation; it's recognition.

5. **Social media as the democratization of narcissism** — everyone can now participate in the cult of self. Result: depression epidemic, suicide spike, global epidemic of meaninglessness. The technology that should connect us has isolated us.

6. **The solution: rediscover humanity** — care about others, put interests of others before your own, sacrifice self-interest for the greater good. This is the peopling thesis: personality is fluid and collaborative.
