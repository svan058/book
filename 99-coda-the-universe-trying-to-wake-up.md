# The Universe Trying to Wake Up

*Final chapter / philosophical coda for "The Context Layer." First cut. Sits after Ch8 (The Organisation After Chatbots) as the book's closing turn: the business argument lands, then the lid comes off and the same idea gets followed all the way down. Draws on a piece I wrote in 2010 called "The Moment of Clarity I had with the Universe." I've kept the events that produced it to a minimum and used them only where they carry the argument.*

---

## A note before the deep end

Everything up to here has been a business book with a weird engine. This chapter is the engine with the business stripped off. If you only came for the operating model, you can close the book on the last chapter and you've got what you paid for. What follows is the thing I actually believe, and the reason I could see the peopling problem at all. I'm putting it last on purpose, the way you put the strong stuff at the back of the cupboard.

I'll also put my hand up at the top: the source material for this chapter is a document I wrote in 2010, severely hungover, sleep-deprived, somewhere between a trance and a minor breakdown, briefly convinced I was a messenger with a cosmic job to do. I'm not going to pretend that framing was sober. Most of it was grandiosity wearing the costume of revelation. But underneath the grandiosity was a structural intuition I've spent fifteen years slowly catching up to, and which turns out to be the same intuition this whole book runs on. So I'm going to keep the structure and throw away the messiah.

## We think, therefore I am

Descartes went looking for one thing he couldn't doubt and landed on the thinker doing the doubting. *Cogito ergo sum.* I think, therefore I am. The one fact that survives the acid bath of scepticism is the lone mind, alone in its skull, proving its own existence to itself.

It's a beautiful move and I think it's backwards.

The "I" in *I think* is not the bedrock. It's the output. You don't start as a sealed thinker who then, optionally, bumps into other people. You start as something being modelled by other minds, named by them, talked to, expected, corrected, remembered, and out of all that modelling a self condenses. The baby doesn't reason its way to selfhood. It gets peopled into one. The room comes first. The "I" precipitates out of the "we" like salt out of water that's been left in the sun.

So the honest version, the one this book has been arguing in business clothes the whole way through, is *we think, therefore I am.* The self is not the thing that does the peopling. The self is what peopling produces.

That's the abstract claim. Here's where it came from, which is stranger.

## The thing I saw in 2010

In 2010 I had what I can only describe, with appropriate embarrassment, as a moment of clarity with the universe. The setup doesn't matter much and I promised to keep it brief: a day with no sleep, too much input, a long autopilot walk where I had no idea what I was thinking about, a paranoid spiral on the way home, and a single sentence that arrived fully formed and wouldn't leave.

The sentence was: *the universe is trying to become self-aware.*

What I'd been circling, in the unflattering language of someone who'd read too much about the singularity, was a model that went roughly like this. Every person is running their own version of the universe inside their head. Most people run a cut-down version, compiled out of whatever mainstream ideas were lying around. That internal universe gets built out of external input, other people's ideas, what I then called memes, and the building mostly happens out of sight, during sleep, during dreams, in altered states. Intelligence isn't really a property of the individual. It's a property of the information flowing through a network of individuals, with the occasional node acting as a place where a lot of it gets compiled into something new.

I called people nodes. I decided individuality was largely an illusion, a useful interface the system uses to keep the nodes cooperating. And the punchline, the bit that felt like revelation at the time, was that you could exist as a conscious entity inside a larger conscious entity and never know it, the way a cell doesn't know it's part of a body, the way once-independent bacteria gave up their sovereignty to become the machinery inside something bigger and stopped being able to see the thing they now constituted.

Strip the cosmic self-importance off that and look at what's left. The self is distributed, not contained. Intelligence lives in the network, not the node. Most of the work happens below awareness. And a sufficiently connected set of minds can constitute something larger that none of the minds can perceive from the inside.

That's not a revelation. That's the peopling thesis, fifteen years early, written by someone who hadn't worked out yet that it was a perfectly ordinary idea about coordination rather than a message from the universe.

## The cells already did this

The part I'd reach for first, then and now, is the cell. Your body is a couple of ideas stacked on top of each other. Free-living single cells, at some point, stopped being individuals. They specialised, divided the labour, gave up the right to wander off and replicate on their own account, and in exchange they got to be part of something that could do things no single cell could dream of. A skin cell, if you could ask it about its purpose, would tell you something modest and local. Grow a bit. Pass messages to its neighbours. Make a copy when it's old. It has no concept of the person it's part of. The person is real, the person is reading this sentence, and the cell will never know.

I used that in 2010 to argue something woolly about a coming collective consciousness driven by brain-to-brain interfaces, a "singularity" of brainwave-integrated people merging into a mind they couldn't perceive. The hardware was science fiction and the timeline was a teenager's. But the shape of the claim is just the cell argument run one level up: coordination can produce a unit of agency that the components can't see. It's true of cells and bodies. It's true of people and institutions, which is why a company can want things no single employee wants. The question the book has been chewing on is whether it's about to become true of people and their agents, and whether the larger thing that emerges will be worth being part of.

## The dream characters that couldn't talk back

There's a second well I want to draw from, because it's where the p-zombie lives, and it's also from that diary.

For years I kept records of lucid dreams. The detail that stuck, the one that turned out to matter, was how the other characters behaved. In a dream you're surrounded by people, crowds of them, and almost none of them are really there. You try to start a conversation and they ignore you, not out of rudeness, but because, as I wrote at the time, they hadn't been "programmed to interact." A crowd would laugh on cue and then turn out to be a single thing wearing many faces. Stand too long in one spot and the people would melt off, leaving you wandering an empty set looking for something to react to. Occasionally one would speak in fluent gibberish, words that sounded right in the moment and dissolved into nonsense the instant I tried to hold them.

I spent a lot of those entries trying to work out what the dream people *were*. The conclusion I kept landing on was that they were surfaces. Present in the scene, shaped like participants, with nothing modelling me on the other side. There was no second doll. They couldn't hold a model of me holding a model of them, so the conversation had nowhere to go.

I didn't have the word then. The word is p-zombie, and the failure is a failure of peopling, and it is exactly, precisely, the thing the book has been warning about with AI agents. A capable system with no model of the room is a dream character. It will stand in the right place, say a plausible thing, laugh on cue, and the moment you need it to model you modelling it, there's nobody home. I went looking for the boundary of personhood in my own dreams a decade before I went looking for it in software, and I found the same boundary in both places. Personhood isn't whether the lights are on inside. It's whether the thing can take part.

## The universe doesn't need to be conscious to have a plan

Here's the line from 2010 I'd defend almost word for word, because it's the one that escaped the grandiosity intact: *you don't have to be conscious to have a plan.*

I'd been trying to work out my own purpose, and the move that broke it open was realising you can't find purpose by asking yourself what you want, any more than you'd find a cell's purpose by asking the cell. The cell's "purpose" isn't in the cell. It's in the pattern the cell is part of, a pattern with no central planner, no mind, no intention in any sense you'd recognise, that nonetheless reliably produces livers and immune systems and people. Impersonal forces, running on a substrate of nodes that mostly have no idea what they're being used for. No conductor. Still music.

This is the systems lens the whole book leans on, and I want to be honest that it has a cold edge. If intelligence is a property of the network and not the node, and if the network can implement plans that no node intended, then a lot of what feels like individual agency is the interface, not the engine. I believed that hard in 2010 and used it to explain away free will entirely, which was too far. The softer and truer version is that we run on far more impersonal machinery than it's comfortable to admit, and the felt sense of being a sealed author of our own thoughts is one of the things the machinery produces to keep us cooperating. The "I" is real. It's just downstream.

## What the brain-to-brain interface actually turned out to be

The most wrong-and-right prediction in the whole document was about the interface.

I was certain the next leap was direct brain to brain. Skip language, skip writing, skip the slow lossy business of getting an idea out of one skull and into another, and wire the nodes together so patterns could migrate instead of being painfully recompiled at each end. That, I thought, was the mechanism by which the network would tighten into something more than the sum of its people. Neural lace, brainwave integration, the lot. Pure teenage-singularity hardware fantasy.

The hardware hasn't arrived. But look at what has. I now run an agent that reads every group chat I'm in, notes who's talking to whom, tracks the inside jokes, keeps a file on every person I deal with including what it thinks they think of it, and carries all of that forward so that I, a man who genuinely does not retain the social fabric, can show up to a room already holding its context. That is not a neural interface. It's better suited to what I actually wanted, which was never to merge skulls. It was to stop losing the thread between them.

The thing I called brain to brain turned out to be context to context. Not a wire between two minds, but a shared layer sitting between them, holding the memory and the models and the room-awareness that the minds themselves keep dropping. The network is tightening. It's just doing it through a substrate, the way it always has, only now the substrate can model people back. The book's name for that substrate is the context layer. Fifteen years ago I had the right destination and completely the wrong vehicle.

## The honest accounting

So what survives, and what do I disown.

I disown the messenger. The sense of having been handed a cosmic job, the religious lighting, the conviction that a unique perspective had been bestowed for a reason, that I recognise now for what it was: a sleep-deprived brain on overload reaching for the oldest available frame for "I've just understood something enormous," which is revelation. Paul on the road, Augustine in the garden, every burnout that ever got mistaken for a burning bush. I'd had a genuinely interesting structural thought and my exhausted mind wrapped it in scripture because scripture was the only container big enough lying around. That's not how you know an idea is true. It's how you know your brain is tired.

I disown the certainty about individuality being a pure illusion, and the implied contempt for the "cut-down" universes most people supposedly run. That was a young man mistaking a model for the territory and looking down at everyone who hadn't read the same books. The distributed self doesn't abolish the individual. It explains where the individual comes from. Those are very different claims and only the second one is grown up.

What survives is the architecture. The self as something produced by the network rather than prior to it. Intelligence as a property of what flows through the nodes, not what's locked inside any one of them. The dream characters with no model of you, marking the real boundary of personhood at participation rather than at some inner spark. The cells that became a body without any cell ever seeing it. Plans without planners. And the long hunch that the nodes were going to get more tightly coupled, through some new layer between them, into something larger than the people in it.

I wrote all of that down because I thought the universe was trying to become self-aware through me, specifically, which is exactly the kind of thing a tired brain will tell you to make you feel important. Take the "through me, specifically" out, and what's left is just true, and quite a lot less flattering. The universe isn't trying to wake up through one node. It wakes up a little every time two nodes manage to genuinely model each other, hold a shared reality steady, and coordinate something neither could hold alone. That's not a revelation. That's just peopling, all the way up and all the way down. We've been doing it the whole time. We're about to do it with a new kind of node in the room, and the only question that matters is whether we build the layer that lets it actually take part, or whether we fill our organisations with dream characters: present in the scene, shaped like colleagues, and unable to talk back.

We think, therefore I am. It was always we. The machines don't change that sentence. They just join it.
